Case Study 2: Six Sigma Quality Improvement at Tata Consultancy Services

1. Title

Applying Six Sigma to Reduce Process Errors in IT Service Delivery at TCS

2. Company Background (90 words)

Tata Consultancy Services (TCS) is a global IT services company delivering software development, consulting, and business process services. Although output is intangible, service quality, consistency, and defect reduction are critical for client satisfaction. TCS has adopted Six Sigma as a structured methodology for quality and process improvement across service operations.

3. Business Context (85 words)

Clients increasingly demand predictable delivery timelines, low error rates, and high service reliability. Project delays and rework due to requirement misunderstandings or process lapses have increased operating costs. Traditional quality checks conducted at the end of project phases have proven insufficient to prevent recurring issues.

4. Core Business Problem (105 words)

TCS faces repeated defects in application maintenance projects, such as missed deadlines, rework, and customer escalations. Although teams track errors, root causes are not systematically analyzed. Management believes that defect reduction requires a structured, data-driven approach rather than isolated corrective actions. The challenge is to apply Six Sigma principles effectively in a service environment where processes are less standardized than manufacturing.

5. Key Data and Metrics (120 words)

Project performance data shows:

  • Average defect rate: 4.2 defects per 1,000 service transactions

  • Rework effort accounts for nearly 15% of total project hours

  • Client escalations increased by 18%

  • Process cycle time varies widely across teams

  • Post-delivery defect leakage remains high
    These indicators suggest process instability and lack of root-cause focus.

6. Decision Situation (70 words)

TCS leadership must decide whether to mandate Six Sigma DMAIC projects for high-impact service processes. The decision involves training Black Belts, dedicating resources, and aligning teams around data-driven improvement.

7. Analytical Questions

  1. How does Six Sigma differ from traditional quality inspection?

  2. Why is DMAIC suitable for service process improvement?

  3. What challenges arise in applying Six Sigma to IT services?

  4. How can defect reduction improve both cost and customer satisfaction?

8. Model Answers

  • Six Sigma focuses on root-cause elimination, not just error detection.

  • DMAIC provides a structured roadmap for reducing process variation.

  • Data availability and process definition are key service challenges.

9. Learning Focus

Understanding Six Sigma application beyond manufacturing environments.

Primary Concepts: Six Sigma, DMAIC, service quality, defect reduction, process improvement

Case Study 3: Lean, JIT, and Kanban at Toyota Kirloskar Motor

1. Title

Lean Production and Just-in-Time Operations at Toyota Kirloskar Motor

2. Company Background (90 words)

Toyota Kirloskar Motor (TKM) operates automobile manufacturing plants in India, following the Toyota Production System (TPS). The company emphasizes Lean production, Just-in-Time (JIT), Kanban, and waste elimination. Operations are designed to produce high quality at low cost with minimal inventory.

3. Business Context (85 words)

Supply chain disruptions and fluctuating demand have increased pressure on inventory management. Holding excess inventory increases cost, while shortages disrupt production. TKM must maintain smooth production flow while minimizing inventory buffers. Lean principles are tested under uncertain operating conditions.

4. Core Business Problem (105 words)

TKM has experienced intermittent production stoppages due to supplier delays and demand variability. While JIT minimizes inventory, it also reduces protection against uncertainty. Management must evaluate whether existing Kanban systems and supplier coordination are sufficient or if additional buffers or process changes are required. The challenge lies in balancing Lean efficiency with operational resilience.

5. Key Data and Metrics (120 words)

Operations data shows:

  • Inventory turnover among the highest in the industry

  • Line stoppages increased by 9% due to part shortages

  • Supplier delivery adherence dropped slightly

  • Space utilization improved due to low inventory levels

  • Cost savings achieved through waste reduction initiatives
    These figures reflect both benefits and risks of Lean systems.

6. Decision Situation (65 words)

Operations leadership must decide whether to modify Kanban quantities or invest in supplier capability improvement. The decision impacts cost, continuity, and Lean discipline.

7. Analytical Questions

  1. How do JIT and Kanban support Lean production?

  2. What risks arise from minimal inventory systems?

  3. How does Theory of Constraints apply to line stoppages?

  4. How can Lean systems be made more resilient?

8. Model Answers

  • Kanban regulates flow and prevents overproduction.

  • Low inventory increases vulnerability to supply disruptions.

  • TOC helps identify and manage production bottlenecks.

9. Learning Focus

Evaluating Lean, JIT, Kanban, and TOC in real manufacturing systems.

Primary Concepts: Lean production, JIT, Kanban, Theory of Constraints, waste reduction
