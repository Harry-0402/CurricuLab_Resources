5. Methods of Manufacturing

Introduction

A method of manufacturing refers to the way in which production activities are organized and executed to convert inputs into finished products.

The choice of manufacturing method depends on:

  • Volume of production

  • Variety of products

  • Nature of demand

  • Degree of customization

Major Methods of Manufacturing

  1. Project / Jobbing Production

  2. Batch Production

  3. Flow / Mass Production

  4. Continuous / Process Production

(Methods 1 & 2 are covered in this part.)

6. Project Production

Introduction

Project production is used when the product is unique, large, and complex, and production is carried out only once or in very small numbers.

Key Concepts

  • One-time production

  • High customization

  • Fixed position layout

  • Long duration

Characteristics of Project Production

  1. Unique Product

    • Each project is different.

  2. Low Volume

    • Usually single-unit production.

  3. Fixed Position Layout

    • Product remains stationary.

    • Men, machines, and materials move.

  4. High Degree of Planning

    • Scheduling and coordination are critical.

  5. Skilled Workforce

    • Requires specialized skills.

Examples

  • Construction of buildings and bridges

  • Shipbuilding

  • Aircraft manufacturing

  • Large infrastructure projects

Advantages of Project Production

AdvantageExplanation
High flexibilityCustom designs
Customer satisfactionTailor-made product
Quality focusIndividual attention

Limitations of Project Production

Management complexityPlanning & control difficult

Summary

Project production is suitable for large, customized, one-time projects.

9. Flow Production (Mass Production)

Introduction

Flow production, also known as mass production, is a method in which large quantities of standardized products are produced continuously using a product (line) layout.

Key Concepts

  • High volume

  • Low variety

  • Standardization

  • Assembly line

Characteristics of Flow Production

  1. Standardized Products

    • Same product produced continuously.

  2. High Volume of Output

    • Large quantities produced.

  3. Product Layout

    • Machines arranged in the sequence of operations.

  4. Special-Purpose Machines

    • Machines designed for specific tasks.

  5. Low Unit Cost

    • Due to economies of scale.

  6. Continuous Flow of Material

    • No interruption between operations.

Examples

  • Automobile assembly

  • Consumer electronics

  • Bottled beverages

  • Household appliances

Text-Based Flow Representation

Limitations of Flow Production

LimitationExplanation
Very low flexibilityStandard products only

Breakdown impactEntire line stops
Worker monotonyRepetitive tasks

Summary

Flow production is ideal for high-demand, standardized products.

10. Continuous Production (Process Production)

Introduction

Continuous production, also called process production, is a method where production runs 24/7 without interruption, and output is produced in a continuous stream.

Key Concepts

  • Continuous flow

  • Highly automated

  • Process industries

  • Capital-intensive

Characteristics of Continuous Production

  1. Continuous Operation

    • Production does not stop.

  2. Highly Automated

    • Minimal human intervention.

  3. Product Standardization

    • No product variety.

  4. Process Layout

    • Based on production process stages.

  5. High Capital Investment

    • Expensive plants and equipment.

  6. Strict Quality Control

    • Any deviation affects entire output.

Examples

  • Oil refineries

  • Chemical plants

  • Cement plants

  • Paper mills

Difference: Flow vs Continuous Production

Continuous production suits process industries with constant demand.
