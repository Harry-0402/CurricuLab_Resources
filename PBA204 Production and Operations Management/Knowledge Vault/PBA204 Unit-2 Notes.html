1. Productivity

Introduction

Productivity is a measure of efficiency with which resources are converted into outputs.
In Operations Management, improving productivity is a primary goal, because it directly affects cost, profitability, and competitiveness.

Key Concepts

  • Efficiency

  • Effectiveness

  • Input–Output relationship

  • Partial productivity

  • Total productivity

Definition of Productivity

Productivity = Output / Input

It shows how well resources are utilized.

Types of Productivity

1. Partial Productivity

Productivity measured with respect to one input.

Examples:

  • Labour productivity = Output / Labour hours

  • Machine productivity = Output / Machine hours

2. Total Productivity

Productivity measured using all inputs together.

mathematica
Total Productivity = Total Output / (Labour + Material + Capital + Energy)

Example

If a factory produces 100 units using:

  • Labour cost = ₹500

  • Material cost = ₹1000

ini
Productivity = 100 / (500 + 1000) = 0.066 units per rupee

Importance of Productivity

  1. Reduces Cost of Production

    • Same output with fewer inputs.

  2. Improves Profitability

    • Lower cost → higher margins.

  3. Enhances Competitiveness

    • Better price and quality.

  4. Economic Growth

    • Higher national productivity improves living standards.

  5. Better Resource Utilization

    • Minimizes waste and idle capacity.

Factors Affecting Productivity

FactorExplanation
TechnologyModern machines improve output
Workforce skillSkilled workers perform efficiently
ManagementBetter planning and control
Quality of materialsReduces rework
Work methodsEfficient processes save time

Summary: Productivity

  • Productivity = Efficiency indicator

  • Applies to labour, machines, and systems

  • Core objective of Operations Management

2. Work Study

Introduction

Work Study is a systematic method of analyzing work to improve efficiency and productivity.
It focuses on how work is done and how long it should take.

Key Concepts

  • Method improvement

  • Time measurement

  • Standardization

  • Productivity improvement

Definition of Work Study

Work Study is the systematic examination of methods of carrying out activities so as to improve the effective use of resources and to set standards of performance.

Components of Work Study

Work Study consists of two main techniques:

pgsql
Work Study
   ├── Methods Study
   └── Work Measurement

1. Methods Study

  • Concerned with how work is done

  • Objective: Find the best method of doing a job

2. Work Measurement

  • Concerned with how long a job should take

  • Objective: Establish standard time

Summary Table

AspectMethods StudyWork Measurement
FocusMethod of workTime required
QuestionHow to do?How long?
OutputBest methodStandard time

3. Objectives of Work Study

Introduction

The main objective of work study is to increase productivity by improving work methods and reducing time.

Major Objectives

  1. Improve Productivity

    • Reduce unnecessary motions and delays.

  2. Reduce Cost

    • Efficient use of labor and machines.

  3. Standardize Work

    • Uniform methods for consistency.

  4. Improve Utilization of Resources

    • Men, machines, and materials.

  5. Improve Working Conditions

    • Reduce fatigue and strain.

  6. Establish Fair Standards

    • Scientific basis for wages and incentives.

Summary

  • Work study improves both method and time

  • Benefits management, workers, and customers

4. Scope of Work Study

Introduction

Work study can be applied to any activity—manufacturing or service.

Areas Covered

  1. Manufacturing operations

  2. Office work

  3. Service activities

  4. Maintenance work

  5. Material handling

Scope Table

AreaApplication
FactoryAssembly, machining
OfficeFiling, billing

Summary

  • Work study is universally applicable

  • Not limited to factories

5. Uses of Work Study

Introduction

Work study is used as a management tool for improvement.

Major Uses

  1. Production planning and control

  2. Manpower planning

  3. Cost control

  4. Incentive schemes

  5. Layout improvement

Summary

  • Work study supports better decisions

  • Leads to sustained productivity improvement

Overall Summary of Part 1

  • Productivity measures efficiency of resource utilization

  • Work study is a systematic approach to improve productivity

  • It consists of Methods Study and Work Measurement

  • Objectives focus on efficiency, cost reduction, and standardization

6. Methods Study

Introduction

Methods Study is a systematic technique used to analyze existing methods of doing work and develop improved methods that reduce cost, time, and effort.

It focuses on:

  • Eliminating unnecessary motions

  • Simplifying work

  • Improving efficiency

Key Concepts

  • Method improvement

  • Work simplification

  • Motion economy

  • Standardized procedures

Definition

Methods Study is the systematic recording and critical examination of existing and proposed ways of doing work, in order to develop easier, more effective, and economical methods.

Objectives of Methods Study

  1. Eliminate unnecessary operations

  2. Simplify work processes

  3. Reduce fatigue and effort

  4. Improve utilization of men, machines, and materials

  5. Reduce production cost

7. Steps in Methods Study (SREDIM)

Methods Study follows a logical sequence of steps, often remembered as SREDIM:

mathematica
Select → Record → Examine → Develop → Install → Maintain

1. Select

  • Select the job or process to be studied

  • Priority given to:

    • Bottlenecks

    • High-cost operations

    • Repetitive work

2. Record

  • Record all relevant facts of the present method

  • Use charts and diagrams

3. Examine

  • Critically analyze each activity using questioning technique:

    • What is done?

    • Why is it done?

    • Where?

    • When?

    • Who does it?

    • How is it done?

4. Develop

  • Develop an improved method

  • Consider:

    • Better layout

    • Mechanization

    • Improved tools

5. Install

  • Implement the new method

  • Train workers

6. Maintain

  • Ensure the method is followed

  • Periodic review and improvement

Summary Table: SREDIM

StepPurpose
SelectChoose job
RecordCapture facts
ExamineAnalyze critically
DevelopCreate better method
InstallImplement
MaintainSustain improvement

9. Flow Diagram

Introduction

A Flow Diagram is a drawing of the workplace layout showing the movement of men or materials.

Key Concepts

  • Physical movement

  • Distance traveled

  • Layout efficiency

Purpose of Flow Diagram

  1. Reduce unnecessary movement

  2. Improve plant layout

  3. Reduce material handling cost

Text-Based Representation

Advantages

  • Shows actual movement

  • Helps improve layout

  • Reduces backtracking

11. Work Measurement

Introduction

Work Measurement is concerned with determining the time required to perform a task by a qualified worker, using a specified method, working at a normal pace.

Key Concepts

  • Time study

  • Standard time

  • Performance rating

  • Allowances

  • Normal time

Definition

Work Measurement is the application of techniques to determine the time required for a worker to carry out a task at a defined level of performance.

Objectives of Work Measurement

  1. Determine standard time

  2. Fix fair wages and incentives

  3. Improve planning and scheduling

  4. Balance workloads

  5. Control production costs

12. Elements of Work Measurement

Introduction

Before measuring time, the job is divided into small, manageable parts called elements.

What is an Element?

An element is a distinct part of a job that can be observed, measured, and timed.

Types of Elements

TypeExplanation
ManualPerformed by worker
MachinePerformed by machine
ConstantSame time each cycle
VariableTime varies
RegularOccurs every cycle
OccasionalOccurs irregularly

Importance

  • Accurate time measurement

  • Easy analysis and improvement

13. Time Study

Introduction

Time Study is the most commonly used technique of work measurement.

Steps in Time Study

  1. Select the job

  2. Break job into elements

  3. Observe and record time

  4. Rate worker performance

  5. Add allowances

  6. Determine standard time

14. Performance Rating

Introduction

Workers do not work at the same speed.
Performance rating adjusts the observed time to a normal pace.

Definition

Performance Rating is the assessment of the worker’s speed or efficiency relative to the standard (normal) performance.

Normal Performance

  • Defined as 100% performance

  • Neither fast nor slow

Rating Formula

sql
Normal Time = Observed Time × (Performance Rating / 100)

Example

Observed time = 10 minutes
Rating = 120%

java
Normal Time = 10 × (120/100) = 12 minutes

Summary

  • Rating corrects observed time

  • Makes time fair and realistic

15. Allowances

Introduction

Workers cannot work continuously without breaks.
Allowances are extra times added to normal time.

Types of Allowances

1. Relaxation Allowance

  • For fatigue and personal needs

  • Depends on:

    • Nature of work

    • Working conditions

2. Contingency Allowance

  • For unavoidable delays

  • Examples:

    • Tool adjustments

    • Minor interruptions

3. Special Allowance

  • For special conditions

  • Examples:

    • Start-up time

    • Learning time

Allowance Formula

Allowances are usually expressed as a percentage of normal time.

16. Standard Time

Introduction

Standard Time is the final time allowed to complete a job.

Definition

Standard Time is the time allowed to perform a job at standard performance, including allowances.

Formula for Standard Time

sql
Standard Time = Normal Time × (1 + Allowance %)

Numerical Example

Observed time = 8 minutes
Performance rating = 125%
Allowance = 20%

Step 1: Normal Time

java
Normal Time = 8 × (125/100) = 10 minutes

Step 2: Standard Time

java
Standard Time = 10 × (1 + 0.20)
              = 10 × 1.20
              = 12 minutes

Importance of Standard Time

  1. Production planning

  2. Scheduling

  3. Wage fixation

  4. Cost estimation

  5. Line balancing

17. Synthetic Time Standards

Introduction

Synthetic time standards are used when direct time study is difficult.

Concept

  • Uses pre-determined time values

  • Based on past data

Example

If:

  • Operation A = 2 min

  • Operation B = 3 min

Synthetic time = 5 minutes

Advantages

  • Saves time

  • Useful for repetitive work

Limitations

  • Less accurate than time study

  • Depends on historical data

18. Work Sampling

Introduction

Work Sampling is a statistical technique used to measure the proportion of time spent on different activities.

Key Concepts

  • Random observations

  • Probability-based

  • Activity distribution

Uses

  • Measure machine utilization

  • Measure worker idle time

  • Service sector analysis

Advantages

  • Less costly

  • Suitable for long-cycle jobs

Limitations

  • Less accurate for short cycles

  • Requires large observations
