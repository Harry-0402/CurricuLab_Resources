1. Inspection

Introduction

Inspection is a quality control technique used to determine whether a product or service meets specified standards.

Inspection helps in:

  • Detecting defects

  • Maintaining quality

  • Protecting customer interests

However, inspection does not improve quality—it only detects defects.

Key Concepts

  • Conformance to specifications

  • Defect detection

  • Quality assurance

  • Acceptance and rejection

Definition of Inspection

Inspection is the process of measuring, examining, or testing a product or service to determine its conformity to specified requirements.

Objectives of Inspection

  1. Ensure products meet quality standards

  2. Prevent defective products from reaching customers

  3. Maintain consistency in production

  4. Provide feedback for corrective action

Place of Inspection in Production

Inspection may be carried out:

  • At incoming stage (raw materials)

  • During production (process inspection)

  • After production (final inspection)

2. Types of Inspection

Inspection is broadly classified into:

sql
Inspection
   ├── Cent Percent Inspection
   └── Sample Inspection

3. Cent Percent Inspection

Introduction

In cent percent inspection, every item produced is inspected.

Key Concepts

  • 100% checking

  • Zero tolerance

  • Complete examination

Characteristics of Cent Percent Inspection

  1. Each unit is inspected

  2. No sampling involved

  3. Defective items are separated

  4. Used when quality is critical

When Cent Percent Inspection is Used

  1. When quality requirements are very strict

  2. When inspection is inexpensive

  3. When failure is dangerous

  4. When volume is small

Examples

  • Aircraft components

  • Medical devices

  • Defense equipment

Advantages of Cent Percent Inspection

AdvantageExplanation
Maximum assuranceAll items checked
No sampling riskNo acceptance error
Suitable for critical itemsSafety ensured

Limitations of Cent Percent Inspection

LimitationExplanation
High costTime and labor intensive
Inspector fatigueHuman errors possible
Not feasible for large volumesImpractical

Summary

Cent percent inspection provides maximum quality assurance but is costly and impractical for mass production.

4. Sample Inspection

Introduction

In sample inspection, only a small sample is inspected from a large lot.

Based on sample results, the entire lot is accepted or rejected.

Key Concepts

  • Sampling

  • Probability

  • Acceptance criteria

  • Quality risk

Characteristics of Sample Inspection

  1. Inspection based on sample

  2. Economical and faster

  3. Uses statistical methods

  4. Risk involved

When Sample Inspection is Used

  1. Large production volumes

  2. Inspection is destructive

  3. Time or cost constraints

  4. Mass production systems

Examples

  • Electrical components

  • Consumer goods

  • Food products

Advantages of Sample Inspection

AdvantageExplanation
Low costFewer items inspected
Less timeFaster decisions
Less fatigueReduced errors
Feasible for mass productionPractical

Limitations of Sample Inspection

LimitationExplanation
Risk involvedWrong acceptance/rejection
Less assuranceNot all items checked
Requires statistical knowledgeComplex planning

Summary

Sample inspection balances cost and quality risk, making it suitable for large-scale production.

5. Operating Characteristic (OC) Curve

Introduction

The Operating Characteristic (OC) Curve shows the performance of a sampling plan.

It represents the probability of accepting a lot for different levels of quality.

Key Concepts

  • Probability of acceptance

  • Lot quality

  • Sampling plan

  • Consumer’s and producer’s risk

Definition

An OC curve is a graph showing the probability of acceptance of a lot against the percentage of defective items in the lot.

Axes of OC Curve

  • X-axis: Percentage of defective items

  • Y-axis: Probability of acceptance

Interpretation of OC Curve

  • Steep curve → Better discrimination

  • Flat curve → Poor discrimination

  • As defects increase → Acceptance probability decreases

Text-Based Representation

markdown
Probability of Acceptance
|
|\
| \
|  \
|   \
|____\______________ % Defectives

Importance of OC Curve

  1. Evaluates effectiveness of sampling plan

  2. Helps balance quality and cost

  3. Shows risk levels

Producer’s and Consumer’s Risk

  • Producer’s Risk (α):

    • Risk of rejecting a good lot

  • Consumer’s Risk (β):

    • Risk of accepting a bad lot

Summary

OC curve helps management understand and control quality risks in sample inspection.

Overall Summary of Part 1

  • Inspection ensures conformance to quality standards

  • Cent percent inspection is thorough but costly

  • Sample inspection is economical but risky

  • OC curve evaluates sampling effectiveness

6. Statistical Quality Control (SQC)

Introduction

Statistical Quality Control uses statistical techniques to monitor, control, and improve quality during production.

Unlike inspection (which detects defects), SQC focuses on preventing defects by controlling the process.

Key Concepts

  • Process variation

  • Statistical methods

  • Control limits

  • Prevention over detection

Definition

Statistical Quality Control is the application of statistical techniques to measure and analyze variation in processes so as to maintain quality standards.

Objectives of SQC

  1. Monitor production process

  2. Detect assignable causes of variation

  3. Maintain consistent quality

  4. Reduce inspection cost

  5. Improve process capability

7. Variation in Quality

Introduction

No two products are exactly identical. Variation is inevitable in any process.

Types of Variation

1. Chance (Random) Causes

  • Natural variation

  • Always present

  • Process is said to be under control

2. Assignable Causes

  • Due to specific reasons:

    • Machine defect

    • Poor material

    • Operator error

  • Process becomes out of control

Summary Table

TypeNatureAction
ChanceRandomAccept
AssignableSpecificCorrect

8. Control Charts

Introduction

A control chart is a graphical tool used to determine whether a process is in control or out of control.

Definition

A control chart is a statistical tool that plots process data over time against control limits to detect variations.

Components of Control Chart

  1. Central Line (CL) – Average value

  2. Upper Control Limit (UCL)

  3. Lower Control Limit (LCL)

UCL
 |
 |        x
 |   x
 |------ CL -------
 |     x
 |   x
 |
LCL

Interpretation

  • Points within limits → Process in control

  • Points outside limits → Assignable cause present

9. Control Charts for Variables

Introduction

Variable charts are used when quality characteristics are measurable, such as:

  • Length

  • Weight

  • Diameter

Types of Variable Charts

  1. X̄ (Mean) Chart

  2. R (Range) Chart

These charts are used together.

9.1 X̄ Chart (Mean Chart)

Purpose

  • Monitors process average

Control Limits for X̄ Chart

ini
UCL = X̄̄ + A₂R̄
CL  = X̄̄
LCL = X̄̄ − A₂R̄

Where:

  • X̄̄ = Average of sample means

  • R̄ = Average of ranges

  • A₂ = Control chart constant

Interpretation

  • Shift in mean indicates process drift

9.2 R Chart (Range Chart)

Purpose

  • Monitors process variability

Control Limits for R Chart

ini
UCL = D₄R̄
CL  = R̄
LCL = D₃R̄

Where:

  • D₃, D₄ = Constants

Importance

  • If variability is out of control, mean chart becomes unreliable

Summary: X̄–R Charts

ChartControls
X̄ ChartCentral tendency
R ChartDispersion

10. Control Charts for Attributes

Introduction

Attribute charts are used when quality is measured as:

  • Defective / Non-defective

  • Number of defects

Types of Attribute Charts

  1. p-chart

  2. np-chart

  3. c-chart

  4. n-chart (often treated with p-chart context)

10.1 p-Chart (Fraction Defective Chart)

Purpose

  • Used when sample size varies

  • Plots fraction defective

Control Limits

bash
p̄ = Average fraction defective

UCL = p̄ + 3√[p̄(1 − p̄)/n]
LCL = p̄ − 3√[p̄(1 − p̄)/n]

Use

  • Widely used in mass production

10.2 np-Chart (Number of Defectives Chart)

Purpose

  • Used when sample size is constant

  • Plots number of defectives

Control Limits

ini
UCL = np̄ + 3√[np̄(1 − p̄)]
CL  = np̄
LCL = np̄ − 3√[np̄(1 − p̄)]

10.3 c-Chart (Number of Defects Chart)

Purpose

  • Used when counting defects per unit

  • Sample size constant

Control Limits

ini
UCL = c̄ + 3√c̄
CL  = c̄
LCL = c̄ − 3√c̄

10.4 n-Chart

  • Used when number of defects per inspection unit is constant

  • Less commonly used separately (often merged with c-chart concepts)

12. Introduction to Six Sigma

Introduction

Six Sigma is a data-driven quality management approach aimed at reducing defects and variability in processes.

It strives for near-perfect quality by minimizing process variation.

Key Concepts

  • Defect reduction

  • Process capability

  • Statistical thinking

  • Customer focus

Definition

Six Sigma is a disciplined, data-driven methodology for eliminating defects and improving process performance.

Meaning of Six Sigma

  • “Sigma (σ)” represents standard deviation

  • Six Sigma quality means:

    • Only 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO)

Objectives of Six Sigma

  1. Reduce process variation

  2. Improve customer satisfaction

  3. Lower cost of poor quality

  4. Improve consistency and reliability

13. Six Sigma Methodology – DMAIC

Six Sigma follows the DMAIC cycle:

powershell
Define → Measure → Analyze → Improve → Control

1. Define

  • Identify problem

  • Define customer requirements

  • Set improvement goals

2. Measure

  • Measure current process performance

  • Collect data

3. Analyze

  • Identify root causes of defects

  • Use statistical tools

4. Improve

  • Implement solutions

  • Optimize process

5. Control

  • Monitor process

  • Sustain improvements

Summary

DMAIC ensures systematic and continuous improvement.

14. Lean Production System

Introduction

Lean production focuses on eliminating waste (Muda) and maximizing value from the customer’s perspective.

Lean aims to produce more with less.

Key Concepts

  • Waste elimination

  • Value stream

  • Continuous improvement

  • Customer value

Definition

Lean Production is a systematic approach to identify and eliminate waste through continuous improvement.

15. Types of Waste (Muda)

Lean identifies seven major types of waste:

Waste TypeExplanation

WaitingIdle time
TransportationUnnecessary movement

InventoryExcess stock

Eliminating waste improves efficiency, quality, and cost.

It focuses on:

JIT means producing the right product, in the right quantity, at the right time.

Key Concepts

Objectives of JIT

  1. Reduce inventory

  2. Improve quality

  3. Reduce lead time

  4. Increase flexibility

18. Kanban System

Introduction

Kanban is a visual control system used to manage material flow in JIT.

Key Concepts

  • Visual signals

  • Pull system

  • Inventory control

Definition

Kanban is a signaling system that authorizes production or movement of items in a pull-based system.

Types of Kanban

  1. Production Kanban

  2. Withdrawal Kanban

Kanban Flow (Text-Based)

nginx
Customer demand → Kanban signal → Production → Delivery

Advantages

  • Simple and visual

  • Prevents overproduction

  • Improves communication

Summary

Kanban ensures smooth flow and inventory control.

19. Theory of Constraints (TOC)

Introduction

The Theory of Constraints, proposed by Eliyahu Goldratt, focuses on identifying and managing system bottlenecks.

Key Concepts

  • Bottleneck

  • Throughput

  • System optimization

Basic Principle

A system’s performance is limited by its weakest constraint.

Five Focusing Steps of TOC

markdown
1. Identify the constraint
2. Exploit the constraint
3. Subordinate other processes
4. Elevate the constraint
5. Repeat the process

Advantages of TOC

  • Improves throughput

  • Reduces work-in-progress

  • Improves delivery performance

Summary

TOC helps organizations focus improvement efforts where they matter most.
