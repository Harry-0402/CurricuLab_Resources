2. Understanding Productivity in Simple Words

2.1 Meaning of Productivity

Productivity is a measure of how efficiently resources are used to produce goods or services.

In simple words:

Productivity shows how much output we get from the inputs we use.

2.2 Basic Formula of Productivity

Productivity=OutputInput\text{Productivity} = \frac{\text{Output}}{\text{Input}}Productivity=InputOutput​

  • Output: Goods or services produced

  • Input: Labour, machines, time, materials, energy, money

2.3 “In Other Words…”

If you can:

  • Produce more output with the same input, or

  • Produce the same output with less input,

then productivity has increased.

3. Types of Productivity

Productivity is not only one type. It can be measured in different ways depending on what input we focus on.

3.1 Labour Productivity

Labour productivity measures output per worker or per labour hour.

Example:
If one worker produces 20 units per day and later produces 30 units per day using the same effort, labour productivity has increased.

Why it matters:
Labour cost is high in most organisations, so improving labour productivity directly reduces cost.

3.2 Machine Productivity

Machine productivity measures how efficiently machines are used.

Example:
If a machine earlier produced 100 units per hour and after maintenance produces 130 units per hour, machine productivity has improved.

3.3 Material Productivity

Material productivity focuses on output per unit of raw material.

Example:
If earlier 10 kg of material produced 50 units and now produces 60 units, material productivity has increased.

3.4 Total Productivity

Total productivity considers all inputs together like labour, machines, materials, power, and capital.

It gives a complete picture of organisational efficiency.

Productivity does not improve automatically. It depends on many factors.

4.1 Human Factors

  • Skill and training of workers

4.2 Method of Work

4.3 Technology and Machines

  • Lack of automation

Better machines generally mean better productivity.

4.4 Management Factors

  • Poor planning

  • Weak supervision

  • Lack of coordination

  • Unrealistic targets

Even good workers fail under bad management.

Normal Time=Observed Time×Performance Rating\text{Normal Time} = \text{Observed Time} \times \text{Performance Rating}Normal Time=Observed Time×Performance Rating

8. Types of Performance Rating Methods

There are four main methods of performance rating commonly discussed in operations management.

8.1 Speed Rating Method

Meaning

Speed rating compares the speed of the worker with the speed of a normal worker.

How it Works

  • Observer judges how fast the worker is working

  • Rating is given as a percentage

Example

If a worker is working faster than normal:

  • Rating = 110% or 120%

Advantages

  • Simple

  • Easy to understand

Limitations

  • Highly subjective

  • Depends on observer’s judgement

8.2 Westinghouse Rating System

This is one of the most popular and detailed rating methods.

Basis of Rating

Worker performance is rated based on four factors:

  1. Skill – Ability to perform the job

  2. Effort – Speed and energy applied

  3. Conditions – Working environment

  4. Consistency – Uniformity of performance

Each factor is given a rating, and the total adjustment is applied to observed time.

Advantages

  • More scientific than speed rating

  • Considers multiple factors

Limitations

  • Complex

  • Requires training

8.3 Synthetic Rating

Meaning

Synthetic rating compares the observed time of an element with a previously established standard time.

Example

If a standard element time is known, the worker’s performance can be rated accordingly.

Advantages

  • Less subjective

  • Based on actual data

Limitations

  • Requires reliable standard data

  • Not suitable for new jobs

8.4 Objective Rating Method

Meaning

Objective rating separates:

  • Work pace

  • Job difficulty

The worker’s speed is rated independently of job difficulty.

Advantages

  • More accurate

  • Reduces bias

Limitations

  • Difficult to apply

  • Requires experience

9. Steps Involved in Performance Rating

Performance rating is carried out systematically.

Step 1: Observe the Worker

The observer carefully watches:

  • Speed

  • Rhythm

  • Smoothness

Step 2: Compare with Normal Performance

The observed performance is compared with a mentally established normal standard.

Step 3: Assign Rating Value

A rating percentage is assigned.

Step 4: Calculate Normal Time

Observed time is multiplied by the rating factor.
