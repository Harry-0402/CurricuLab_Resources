  • Process: manufacturing or service activities

1. Big Picture: Why Did Operations Management Evolve?

Operations Management did not come into existence overnight. It evolved gradually as businesses faced new challenges such as increase in demand, competition, cost pressure, and quality issues. As markets expanded, old methods of production became slow and inefficient. This forced organisations to improve the way work was planned, organised, and controlled.

Think of it like transportation:

  • Walking ‚Üí Bicycle ‚Üí Car ‚Üí Airplane
    Each stage improved speed, capacity, and efficiency.
    Operations Management evolved in the same way.

2. Craft Production System (Early Stage)

This was the earliest form of production.

Main characteristics:

  • Products were made by skilled workers

  • One worker completed the entire product

  • Use of simple hand tools

  • High level of customization

  • Production was slow and expensive

Example:
A carpenter making a table from start to finish alone.

Limitations:

  • Very low output

  • High cost

  • Could not meet large customer demand

This system worked only when demand was small.

3. Factory System

The Industrial Revolution brought a major change called the factory system.

Key features:

  • Workers, machines, and materials brought under one roof

  • Introduction of power-driven machines

  • Division of labour (each worker performs a small task)

  • Better supervision and control

Benefits:

  • Increased production

  • Reduced cost per unit

  • Faster production

Example:
Textile mills producing cloth using machines instead of handlooms.

4. Mass Production System

As demand increased further, the need for large-scale production arose.

Characteristics of mass production:

  • Large volume of identical products

  • Standardised parts

  • Use of assembly line system

  • Low cost per unit

This system was popularised by Henry Ford, who introduced the moving assembly line.

Impact of Henry Ford‚Äôs Contribution:

  • Production time reduced drastically

  • Cost of products reduced

  • Goods became affordable for common people

Example:
Earlier, producing one car took many days. Ford reduced it to a few hours.

5. Scientific Management Approach

As factories became larger, efficiency became a major concern.

Focus of this stage:

  • Improving work methods

  • Reducing unnecessary movements

  • Standardising tasks

  • Increasing worker productivity

The emphasis shifted from what to produce to how to produce efficiently.

6. Quality Movement

Mass production created new problems such as:

  • Defective products

  • High rejection rates

  • Customer complaints

This led to the quality movement.

Experts like W. Edwards Deming stressed:

  • Continuous improvement

  • Statistical quality control

  • Management responsibility for quality

Key idea:

Quality is not achieved only by inspection, but by improving the process itself.

7. Shift from Manufacturing to Operations Management

Over time, businesses realised that:

  • Services are as important as goods

  • Customer satisfaction is critical

  • Speed, flexibility, and reliability matter

This led to the broader concept of Operations Management, which includes:

  • Manufacturing + Services

  • Planning + Execution

  • Cost control + Quality improvement

Operations Management now applies to:

  • Hospitals

  • Banks

  • Airlines

  • Educational institutions

8. Modern Operations Management

Today‚Äôs Operations Management focuses on:

  • Automation and technology

  • Lean production systems

  • Customer-driven operations

  • Continuous improvement

  • Flexibility and quick response

The goal is to achieve:

  • High quality

  • Low cost

  • Fast delivery

  • Customer satisfaction
