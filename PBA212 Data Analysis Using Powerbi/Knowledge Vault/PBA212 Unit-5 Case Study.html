Case Study

Building Actionable Executive Dashboards at Orion Industrial Systems Ltd.: Visual Analytics for Manufacturing Excellence

1. Organizational Background

Orion Industrial Systems Ltd. (OISL) is a large Indian manufacturing organization specializing in industrial equipment, automation components, and heavy engineering assemblies used across automotive, power, and infrastructure sectors. Established in 2003, the company has grown into a multi-plant operation with manufacturing facilities in Pune, Vadodara, Hosur, and Jamshedpur. As of FY2024, OISL reported consolidated revenues of INR 3,950 crore and employed over 8,200 people across production, supply chain, engineering, sales, and corporate functions.

OISL operates a vertically integrated business model. Core activities include in-house machining, fabrication, assembly, and testing, supported by a network of domestic and international suppliers. The company serves more than 1,200 enterprise customers and maintains long-term contracts with several large OEMs. Manufacturing contributes nearly 78% of total operating costs, making operational efficiency critical to profitability.

Over the past five years, OISL invested significantly in digital systems such as ERP, Manufacturing Execution Systems (MES), quality management tools, and warehouse management platforms. These systems generated large volumes of operational data related to production output, machine utilization, defect rates, inventory movement, and order fulfillment. To make sense of this data, the company adopted Power BI as its enterprise analytics and dashboarding platform.

Initially, dashboards were built independently by different teams to meet local reporting needs. Over time, however, senior management recognized the need for standardized, executive-level dashboards that could provide a clear, consistent, and actionable view of business performance across plants and functions.

2. Business Environment and Context

The manufacturing sector in India has become increasingly competitive, driven by cost pressures, global supply chain disruptions, and rising customer expectations for quality and delivery reliability. Customers now demand shorter lead times, real-time order visibility, and strict adherence to service-level agreements. Failure to meet these expectations often results in penalties, loss of contracts, or reduced margins.

At the same time, operational complexity has increased. Multi-plant operations, diverse product lines, and fluctuating demand patterns require managers to monitor performance across multiple dimensions. Traditional static reports are no longer sufficient. Executives require visual dashboards that summarize key metrics while allowing drill-down into problem areas.

OISL's leadership emphasized the importance of visual analytics to support daily operational reviews, weekly performance meetings, and monthly strategic planning. However, they also observed that poorly designed dashboards could overwhelm users, highlight irrelevant metrics, or even lead to incorrect conclusions. This made dashboard design and visualization strategy a critical managerial concern rather than a purely technical task.

3. Emerging Business Problem

As Power BI adoption expanded at OISL, the number of dashboards increased rapidly. Plant managers tracked production and quality metrics, supply chain teams monitored inventory and logistics, while finance focused on cost and margin analysis. Each dashboard used different visual styles, color schemes, and KPI definitions.

Executives reported difficulty in interpreting dashboards during review meetings. Some dashboards displayed too many visuals without clear hierarchy, while others focused on operational details without highlighting strategic KPIs. Critical issues such as declining OEE or rising defect trends were sometimes buried under excessive charts and tables.

Inconsistent visualization choices also caused confusion. For example, cumulative production targets were shown using pie charts, trend analysis was attempted with stacked bars, and comparisons across plants lacked standard scales. Interactivity features such as slicers and drill-downs were either underused or misused, leading to misinterpretation of results.

Management realized that while data and calculations were largely correct, the way insights were visualized and communicated was limiting decision effectiveness. The core problem was the absence of a structured visualization and dashboarding framework aligned with managerial needs.

4. Detailed Data and Metrics

OISL consolidated operational data from its four plants for FY2023‚ÄìFY2024. Monthly production output ranged from 48,000 to 62,000 units across all plants. Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE) averaged 71%, with Plant Hosur consistently outperforming others at 78%, while Jamshedpur lagged at around 65%.

Defect rates varied between 1.8% and 3.9% depending on product category and plant. Inventory turnover ranged from 6.2 to 9.1 annually, reflecting uneven demand planning and material flow. Order fulfillment lead times averaged 14 days but exceeded 20 days during peak demand periods.

Key KPIs identified by leadership included production volume vs target, OEE, defect rate, inventory days, on-time-in-full (OTIF) delivery, manufacturing cost per unit, and contribution margin. These KPIs were available in data models but were not consistently highlighted or prioritized across dashboards.

The challenge was not lack of metrics, but selecting the right metrics, choosing appropriate visual representations, and presenting them in a manner that supported fast, confident decision-making.

5. Internal Challenges

Several internal constraints affected OISL's ability to build effective dashboards. Business users had varying levels of data literacy and often requested additional visuals without considering clarity or relevance. Analysts focused on technical correctness rather than storytelling and usability.

There were no formal design standards for dashboards. Color usage, chart types, and layout structures differed widely. Some dashboards attempted to serve both executives and operational staff, resulting in conflicting design objectives.

Additionally, governance around dashboard publishing and sharing was weak. Multiple versions of similar dashboards existed, increasing confusion and reducing trust. Training efforts had focused on tool usage rather than principles of effective visualization.

6. Analytical Context

OISL's analytics leadership proposed a redesign of dashboards based on visualization best practices and managerial use cases. This involved defining different dashboard types for executives, plant managers, and functional heads.

Executive dashboards would focus on high-level KPIs, trends, and exceptions using a limited number of visuals. Operational dashboards would allow detailed drill-downs, filters, and root-cause analysis. Visual choices would align with data characteristics‚Äîline charts for trends, bar charts for comparisons, matrices for detailed views, and KPI cards for headline metrics.

Interactivity features such as slicers, drill-throughs, and tooltips would be used selectively to enhance exploration without overwhelming users. The objective was to turn dashboards into decision-support tools rather than static reporting artifacts.

7. Decision Point

The executive committee must decide whether to implement a standardized dashboarding framework across OISL. This includes defining visualization standards, approving KPI hierarchies, segmenting dashboards by user role, and establishing governance for dashboard publication and sharing.

The decision involves balancing flexibility for analysts with consistency for decision-makers. Poorly executed standardization could stifle innovation, while lack of standards could continue to dilute the impact of analytics investments.

8. Analytical and Decision Questions

  1. What visualization issues reduced the effectiveness of dashboards at OISL?
  2. How should executive dashboards differ from operational dashboards in manufacturing?
  3. Which KPIs deserve priority placement on executive dashboards and why?
  4. How does choice of visual type influence managerial interpretation?
  5. What role should interactivity play in executive decision-making dashboards?
  6. How can dashboard clutter be reduced without losing critical insights?
  7. What governance controls are necessary for dashboard sharing and version control?
  8. How should the impact of improved dashboards be measured?

9. Model Answers (Selected)

Question 1: Poor visual hierarchy, excessive charts, and inappropriate chart types made dashboards hard to interpret, reducing decision speed and clarity.

Question 2: Executive dashboards should emphasize summary KPIs and trends, while operational dashboards should support detailed analysis and root-cause investigation.

Question 3: KPIs such as OEE, defect rate, OTIF, and contribution margin should be prioritized due to their direct impact on profitability and customer satisfaction.

Question 4: Appropriate visual selection ensures patterns and comparisons are immediately visible, preventing misinterpretation of data.

10. Learning Focus

This case develops students' ability to design effective dashboards that translate data into action. It emphasizes visualization strategy, KPI storytelling, and managerial decision-making in manufacturing contexts.
