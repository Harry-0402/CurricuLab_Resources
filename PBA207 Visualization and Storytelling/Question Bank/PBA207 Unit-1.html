1️⃣ The Big Picture – What Does “How to Visualize” Really Mean?

Many students think visualization simply means:

“Put data in Excel and click Insert Chart.”

That is not visualization.

That is chart creation.

Real visualization means:

👉 Understanding your message
👉 Choosing the right visual
👉 Designing it clearly
👉 Communicating insight

Think of it like cooking.

Data = Raw vegetables
Chart = Cooking process
Insight = Final dish

If you don’t cook properly, even good ingredients taste bad.

2️⃣ Step-by-Step Process of Effective Data Visualization

Let’s break it into practical steps.

🔹 Step 1: Understand the Objective

Before creating any chart, ask:

  • Who is the audience?

  • What decision needs to be made?

  • What message should they understand?

If you don’t know the purpose, your chart will confuse people.

Example:
If the goal is to show growth over time → use a line graph.
If the goal is comparison → use a bar chart.

Purpose decides chart type.

🔹 Step 2: Understand the Data

You must understand:

  • What type of data is it?

    • Numerical?

    • Categorical?

    • Time-based?

  • How many variables are involved?

  • Is the data clean?

If data has errors, the visualization will mislead.

Garbage in → Garbage out.

🔹 Step 3: Choose the Right Chart Type

Different charts serve different purposes.

ObjectiveSuitable Chart
ComparisonBar chart
Trend over timeLine chart
DistributionHistogram
RelationshipScatter plot
CompositionPie or stacked bar

Choosing the wrong chart creates confusion.

For example:
Showing trends using pie chart → wrong.
Using line chart for categories → wrong.

Chart must match purpose.

🔹 Step 4: Keep It Simple

This is very important.

Avoid:
❌ Too many colors
❌ 3D effects
❌ Too many labels
❌ Unnecessary background

Remember:
👉 Simple visuals communicate better.

Your goal is clarity, not decoration.

🔹 Step 5: Highlight Important Information

Not all data points are equally important.

Use:

  • Bold titles

  • Different color for key data

  • Annotations

  • Data labels (if necessary)

Guide your audience to what matters.

Don’t make them search for the insight.

🔹 Step 6: Add Clear Titles and Labels

Every visualization must answer:

  • What is this about?

  • What are the units?

  • What time period?

Example:
Wrong title: “Sales Data”
Better title: “Monthly Sales Growth from 2021 to 2023”

Specific titles improve understanding.

🔹 Step 7: Test the Visualization

Before presenting, ask:

  • Can someone understand this in 10 seconds?

  • Is the message clear?

  • Is there unnecessary clutter?

If explanation takes too long, simplify it.

Now instead of just listing numbers, you place each pair of values as a dot on a graph.

That is a scatter plot.

A scatter plot shows the relationship between two numerical variables using dots.

Each dot represents one observation.

Simple idea:

👉 It helps you see whether two variables are connected.

2️⃣ Structure of a Scatter Plot

A scatter plot has:

  • X-axis → Independent variable (e.g., Advertising cost)

  • Y-axis → Dependent variable (e.g., Sales)

  • Dots → Data points

Example:

Advertising (₹)Sales (₹)
10,0001,00,000
15,0001,30,000
20,0001,60,000

When plotted:
Dots move upward.

This shows positive relationship.

3️⃣ Types of Relationships in Scatter Plot

Scatter plots help identify:

🔹 1. Positive Relationship

When one variable increases, the other also increases.

Example:
Higher advertising → Higher sales.

Dots move upward.

🔹 2. Negative Relationship

When one variable increases, the other decreases.

Example:
Higher price → Lower demand.

Dots move downward.

🔹 3. No Relationship

No clear pattern.

Dots scattered randomly.

This means variables are unrelated.

4️⃣ When Should You Use a Scatter Plot?

Best used for:

✔ Studying correlation
✔ Analyzing cause-effect relationship
✔ Identifying patterns
✔ Detecting outliers

  • Experience vs Salary

  • Temperature vs Electricity consumption

5️⃣ When Should You Avoid Scatter Plot?

❌ When data is categorical
❌ When only one variable exists
❌ When showing trend over time (line chart better)
❌ When comparing categories (bar chart better)

Scatter plot is strictly for numerical relationships.
