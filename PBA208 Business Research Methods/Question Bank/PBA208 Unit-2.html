❓ Question: What is Experimental Research Design?

✅ Answer:

Experimental research design is used to find cause-and-effect relationships between variables.

Think of it like testing a new recipe. If you change one ingredient and keep everything else the same, you can see how that one change affects the taste. That’s exactly what experimental research does — it changes one factor and studies its effect.

In other words, it helps answer questions like: Does X cause Y?

Example:
If a company changes its advertisement style to see whether sales increase, it is using experimental research design.

❓ Question: What is Pre-Experimental Design?

✅ Answer:

Pre-experimental design is the simplest type of experimental design where there is little or no control over variables.

Think of it like trying a new teaching method in one class and seeing the result, but without comparing it to another class. Since there is no proper comparison group, the results may not be fully reliable.

In other words, it studies the effect of a treatment, but without strong control or random selection.

Example:
A company gives sales training to one group of employees and checks their performance afterward, without comparing it to another group.

❓ Question: What is Quasi-Experimental Design?

✅ Answer:

Quasi-experimental design is similar to experimental design, but it does not use random selection of participants.

Think of it like comparing two existing classrooms — one uses smart boards and the other does not. You did not randomly assign students to each class, but you still compare the results.

In other words, it studies cause and effect, but without full control over all factors.

Example:
A company compares performance between two branches where one branch naturally adopted a new strategy and the other did not.

❓ Question: What is True-Experimental Design?

✅ Answer:

True-experimental design is the most accurate type of experimental design because it uses random selection and a control group.

Think of it like a fair game where names are picked randomly. This makes sure no one gets special treatment. In research, random selection helps remove bias and gives more reliable results.

In other words, true-experimental design studies cause and effect with full control over variables.

Example:
A company randomly divides employees into two groups — one gets new training and the other does not — and then compares their performance.

❓ Question: Explain Research Design in detail, including its types and importance.

✅ Answer:

1️⃣ The Big Picture – What is Research Design?

Think of research design like a blueprint of a building.

Before building a house, an engineer prepares a detailed plan — where the rooms will be, how strong the walls will be, what materials will be used, and how everything connects. Without this plan, the house may collapse or cost too much.

In the same way, research design is the overall plan of a research study. It tells us:

  • What data to collect

  • From whom to collect

  • How to collect

  • How to analyze

  • How to test the results

In simple words, research design is the roadmap that guides the whole research process from start to finish.

2️⃣ Why Research Design is Important

Imagine starting a road trip without Google Maps. You may waste fuel, time, and energy. Research without design is the same — confused, costly, and unreliable.

Research design is important because:

  • It saves time and money

  • It avoids confusion

  • It reduces errors and bias

  • It increases accuracy

  • It helps in proper testing of hypothesis

  • It ensures data is relevant

Without research design, research becomes random guessing.

3️⃣ Functions of Research Design

Let’s understand its main functions in simple language.

1. It Gives Direction

It clearly defines what the researcher wants to study.

2. It Controls Variables

It helps control unwanted factors that may affect results.

3. It Improves Accuracy

It ensures the right method is used to collect data.

4. It Organizes Resources

It helps manage time, cost, and manpower.

5. It Helps in Hypothesis Testing

It makes sure the study can properly test cause-and-effect relationships.

In short, research design keeps the study focused and systematic.

4️⃣ Types of Research Design

There are three main types:

A. Exploratory Research Design

What is it?

Used when the problem is not very clear.

Think of it like trying to understand why your phone battery drains fast. First, you explore possible reasons — apps, brightness, background activity, etc.

Features:

  • Flexible

  • Informal

  • Small sample size

  • Helps generate ideas

Methods Used:

  • Interviews

  • Focus groups

  • Case studies

Example:

If a company notices sales are dropping but does not know why, it conducts informal interviews with customers.

B. Descriptive Research Design

What is it?

Used to describe characteristics of a group or situation.

Think of it like taking a photograph. It captures what exists but does not explain why.

Features:

  • Structured

  • Large sample

  • Quantitative data

  • Surveys and questionnaires

Example:

A study that finds 60% customers prefer online shopping over offline shopping.

C. Experimental Research Design

What is it?

Used to study cause-and-effect relationships.

Think of it like testing a new fertilizer on plants. You apply it to one group and compare with another group.

Key Elements:

  • Independent variable (cause)

  • Dependent variable (effect)

  • Control group

  • Random selection

5️⃣ Types of Experimental Design

Experimental design can be divided into four types:

1. Pre-Experimental Design

  • Very simple

  • No proper control group

  • Results may not be fully reliable

Example: Giving training to one group and checking performance without comparison.

2. Quasi-Experimental Design

  • Has comparison group

  • No random assignment

Example: Comparing two company branches where one adopted a new strategy.

3. True-Experimental Design

  • Has control group

  • Uses random assignment

  • Most accurate

Example: Randomly dividing employees into two groups to test training impact.

4. Statistical Design

  • Uses statistical tools

  • More complex

  • Helps analyze multiple variables

Example: Using ANOVA to compare performance across different groups.

6️⃣ Validity of Research Instruments

When we design research, we must ensure tools like questionnaires are valid.

Validity means the tool measures what it is supposed to measure.

There are three main types:

A. Face Validity

It looks correct on the surface.

Example: Customer satisfaction survey asking about service quality.

B. Content Validity

It covers all important areas of the topic.

Example: Employee performance survey covering skills, teamwork, punctuality.

C. Construct Validity

It measures the actual concept properly.

Example: A motivation test truly measuring motivation, not intelligence.

7️⃣ Comparison of Research Designs

BasisExploratoryDescriptiveExperimental
PurposeExplore problemDescribe situationFind cause & effect
StructureFlexibleStructuredHighly controlled
HypothesisNot requiredMay or may notRequired
ExampleMarket problemCustomer surveyTesting advertisement impact

8️⃣ Business Case Example

Let’s take a real business situation.

Suppose a clothing company faces low sales.

Step 1: Exploratory

They interview customers to understand reasons.

Step 2: Descriptive

They conduct a survey to measure how many customers prefer competitors.

Step 3: Experimental

They test a new discount strategy in one city and compare sales with another city.

See how research design guides every step? Without it, they would just guess.

9️⃣ Advantages of Research Design

  • Reduces risk

  • Improves reliability

  • Saves cost

  • Provides clarity

  • Helps decision-making

🔟 Limitations

  • Requires time to plan

  • May be costly

  • Needs expertise

  • Not always perfect due to real-world limitations

🏁 Final Takeaway

Research design is the foundation of successful research.

Just like a strong blueprint builds a strong building, a good research design builds strong and reliable results.

Without research design, research becomes confusion. With research design, research becomes science.

❓ Question: Explain Validity of Research Instruments in detail.

✅ Answer:

1️⃣ Big Picture – What is Validity?

Imagine you step on a weighing machine to check your weight.

If the machine shows your correct weight, it is valid.
If it shows random numbers, even if it looks fancy, it is not valid.

That’s exactly what validity means in research.

👉 Validity means a research tool measures what it is supposed to measure.

In simple words, validity answers this question:

“Are we measuring the right thing?”

If the tool is not valid, then the whole research becomes useless — no matter how much data we collect.

2️⃣ Why Validity is Important

Think of shooting an arrow at a target.

  • If the arrow hits the center, it’s accurate.

  • If it hits somewhere else, your aim was wrong.

In research:

  • The questionnaire is the arrow.

  • The concept (like satisfaction, motivation, performance) is the target.

If the tool does not measure correctly, conclusions will be wrong. That can lead to bad business decisions.

For example:

If a company uses a wrong tool to measure employee motivation, it may introduce the wrong policies.

So validity protects research from giving false results.

3️⃣ Types of Validity

There are three major types of validity in your syllabus:

  1. Face Validity

  2. Content Validity

  3. Construct Validity

Let’s understand them one by one in simple language.

A. Face Validity

What is Face Validity?

Face validity means the tool looks correct on the surface.

It is about first impression.

Think of it like a maths exam paper.

If the subject is maths but questions are about history, it clearly does not look correct.

So face validity asks:

👉 “Does this tool appear to measure what it claims to measure?”

Example:

If a customer satisfaction survey asks questions like:

  • Are you happy with product quality?

  • Are you satisfied with service?

  • Would you recommend us?

Then it has face validity because the questions look relevant.

But remember:

Face validity is not deep testing. It is only about appearance.

B. Content Validity

What is Content Validity?

Content validity means the tool covers all important parts of the concept.

Think of it like an exam covering all chapters in the syllabus.

If the syllabus has 5 chapters but exam questions come from only 1 chapter, the exam does not have good content validity.

In research:

If we are measuring “employee performance”, we must include:

  • Skills

  • Teamwork

  • Productivity

  • Attendance

  • Communication

If any major area is missing, content validity is weak.

Why It Matters

If important parts are missing, results will be incomplete.

Example:

A performance evaluation form that only measures punctuality but ignores skills and teamwork does not have good content validity.

C. Construct Validity

What is Construct Validity?

This is deeper and more important.

Construct validity checks whether the tool truly measures the actual concept (called “construct”).

Construct means an abstract idea like:

  • Intelligence

  • Motivation

  • Satisfaction

  • Loyalty

These cannot be touched or seen directly.

So we create questions to measure them.

Construct validity asks:

👉 “Is the tool actually measuring the concept, or something else?”

Example:

Suppose we want to measure “motivation”.

If our questions mostly test knowledge or intelligence instead of desire to work, then the tool lacks construct validity.

4️⃣ Comparison of Validity Types

TypeMeaningLevel
Face ValidityLooks correct on surfaceBasic
Content ValidityCovers all important areasModerate
Construct ValidityTruly measures the conceptDeep

Think of it like layers of checking:

  • Face = First look

  • Content = Full coverage

  • Construct = True meaning

5️⃣ Real Business Example

Let’s say a company wants to measure customer loyalty.

Wrong Approach:

Asking only:

  • Do you like our logo?

This is not valid.

Better Approach:

Include questions like:

  • Will you buy again?

  • Will you recommend us?

  • How satisfied are you?

  • How likely are you to switch brands?

Now the tool measures loyalty properly.

That shows good validity.

6️⃣ How to Improve Validity

Researchers improve validity by:

  • Consulting experts

  • Reviewing literature

  • Conducting pilot testing

  • Making revisions

  • Removing unclear questions

Before final data collection, tools are tested and improved.

7️⃣ Advantages of Valid Research Tools

  • Accurate results

  • Better decision-making

  • Strong conclusions

  • Higher research quality

8️⃣ Limitations

  • Checking validity takes time

  • Requires expert knowledge

  • Sometimes difficult to measure abstract concepts

🏁 Final Takeaway

Validity is the heart of good research.

If the tool does not measure the right thing, the whole research fails — no matter how advanced the analysis is.

In simple words:

👉 Validity means measuring the right thing, in the right way.

❓ Question: Explain Methods of Data Collection in detail.

✅ Answer:

1️⃣ Big Picture – What is Data Collection?

Imagine you are a doctor.

Before giving medicine, you must first check the patient — temperature, blood pressure, symptoms. If you don’t collect proper information, you may give the wrong treatment.

Research works the same way.

👉 Data collection means gathering information needed to solve a research problem.

If data is wrong, incomplete, or biased, then the research conclusion will also be wrong.

So data collection is one of the most important steps in research design.

2️⃣ Types of Data

Before understanding methods, we must understand two types of data:

1. Primary Data

This is new data collected for the first time by the researcher.

Example: Conducting a survey to know customer satisfaction.

2. Secondary Data

This is already collected data by someone else.

Example: Government reports, company records, published research.

Primary data is more specific but costly.
Secondary data is cheaper but may not perfectly match the problem.

3️⃣ Methods of Collecting Primary Data

There are mainly five methods:

  1. Observation

  2. Personal Interview

  3. Telephone Interview

  4. Questionnaire (Mail/Online)

  5. Schedule (Enumerator Method)

Let’s understand each one simply.

A. Observation Method

What is it?

In this method, the researcher watches people and records behavior.

Think of a shop owner watching how customers move inside the store.

No questions asked — just observation.

Example:

A supermarket observes which shelves customers visit most.

Advantages:

  • Real behavior recorded

  • No influence from questions

Disadvantages:

  • Expensive

  • Limited information

  • Cannot know thoughts or feelings

B. Personal Interview Method

What is it?

The researcher directly meets the respondent and asks questions.

Think of it like a job interview — face-to-face interaction.

Example:

A bank officer asking customers about service quality.

Advantages:

  • Detailed information

  • Can clarify doubts

  • High response rate

Disadvantages:

  • Time-consuming

  • Expensive

  • Interviewer bias possible

C. Telephone Interview

What is it?

Information is collected through phone calls.

Example:

A company calling customers to ask about feedback.

Advantages:

  • Fast

  • Cheaper than personal interview

  • Suitable for large areas

Disadvantages:

  • Short answers only

  • People may disconnect

  • No observation of body language

D. Questionnaire Method

What is it?

A list of questions is sent to respondents by mail or online.

The researcher does not meet them directly.

Example:

Google Form asking students about online learning experience.

Advantages:

  • Low cost

  • Large sample possible

  • No interviewer bias

Disadvantages:

  • Low response rate

  • No control over who fills it

  • No clarification possible

E. Schedule Method

What is it?

In this method, trained enumerators visit respondents and fill answers themselves.

Think of census officers collecting population data.

Example:

Government census survey.

Advantages:

  • Accurate data

  • High response rate

  • Suitable for less educated respondents

Disadvantages:

  • Very expensive

  • Needs training

  • Time-consuming

4️⃣ Secondary Data Collection

Secondary data comes from:

  • Government publications

  • Research journals

  • Books

  • Company records

  • Websites

  • Reports

Example:

Using RBI reports to study inflation trends.

Advantages:

  • Cheap

  • Time-saving

  • Easily available

Disadvantages:

  • May be outdated

  • May not fit research purpose exactly

5️⃣ Comparison Table

MethodCostAccuracyResponse Rate
ObservationHighModerateNot applicable
Personal InterviewHighHighHigh
TelephoneMediumModerateMedium
QuestionnaireLowModerateLow
ScheduleVery HighVery HighVery High

6️⃣ Choosing the Right Method

Choosing a method depends on:

  • Nature of research

  • Budget

  • Time available

  • Type of respondents

  • Required accuracy

For example:

If the target audience is rural population, schedule method is better.
If the target audience is corporate employees, online questionnaire works well.

7️⃣ Business Case Example

Suppose a food delivery company wants to improve service.

Step 1: Observation

They observe delivery timing patterns.

Step 2: Questionnaire

They send feedback forms to customers.

Step 3: Telephone Interview

They call selected customers for detailed discussion.

See how different methods can be combined?

That is smart research design.

8️⃣ Advantages of Proper Data Collection

  • Better decision-making

  • Accurate conclusions

  • Strong research credibility

  • Helps in hypothesis testing

9️⃣ Limitations

  • Costly

  • Time-consuming

  • Risk of bias

  • Non-response problem

🏁 Final Takeaway

Data collection is the backbone of research.

If you collect wrong data, even the best statistical tools cannot save the research.

In simple words:

👉 Good data = Good research
👉 Bad data = Bad decisions

Status: Unit 2 | Topic: Attitudinal Scales – Likert Scale & Questionnaire Designing | 15 Marks | Q 4/10

❓ Question: Explain Attitudinal Scales (Likert Scale) and Questionnaire Designing in detail.

✅ Answer:

1️⃣ Big Picture – What are Attitudinal Scales?

In business research, we often need to measure things like:

  • Customer satisfaction

  • Employee motivation

  • Brand loyalty

  • Public opinion

But here’s the problem…

You cannot see or touch satisfaction or loyalty. They are feelings.

So how do we measure them?

👉 We use attitudinal scales.

Think of it like using a thermometer for temperature.
Attitudinal scales are tools to measure people’s opinions and feelings in numbers.

2️⃣ Likert Scale – The Most Popular Attitudinal Scale

What is Likert Scale?

Likert Scale is a method where respondents show their level of agreement or disagreement with a statement.

It was developed by Rensis Likert.

It usually has 5 or 7 response options.

Example of 5-Point Likert Scale

Statement:

“I am satisfied with the company’s customer service.”

Options:

  1. Strongly Disagree

  2. Disagree

  3. Neutral

  4. Agree

  5. Strongly Agree

Each option is given a numerical value (1 to 5).

This converts feelings into numbers.

That makes analysis easy.

Why Likert Scale is Popular

  • Easy to understand

  • Easy to answer

  • Easy to analyze

  • Suitable for large surveys

3️⃣ Features of Likert Scale

  1. Uses statements instead of direct questions

  2. Measures intensity of feeling

  3. Balanced options (positive and negative)

  4. Simple scoring system

Think of it like rating a movie from 1 to 5 stars.

More stars = More satisfaction.

4️⃣ Advantages of Likert Scale

  • Simple and clear

  • Reliable

  • Quick to design

  • Easy for respondents

5️⃣ Limitations of Likert Scale

  • Some people choose “Neutral” without thinking

  • Response bias possible

  • Cultural differences may affect answers

6️⃣ Questionnaire Designing

Now let’s understand how to design a good questionnaire.

Think of questionnaire designing like cooking.

If ingredients are wrong, food tastes bad.

If questions are badly designed, data becomes useless.

Steps in Questionnaire Designing

Step 1: Define Objective Clearly

Before writing questions, ask:

👉 What exactly do I want to measure?

If objective is unclear, questions will also be unclear.

Step 2: Choose Question Type

There are two types:

  1. Open-ended questions
    Example: “What do you think about our service?”

  2. Closed-ended questions
    Example: “Are you satisfied? Yes/No”

Closed-ended questions are easier to analyze.

Step 3: Use Simple Language

Avoid technical words.

Instead of:
“Evaluate the operational efficiency.”

Write:
“How satisfied are you with our service speed?”

Keep language simple and direct.

Step 4: Avoid Leading Questions

Wrong question:
“Don’t you think our product is excellent?”

Correct question:
“How would you rate our product quality?”

Leading questions create biased answers.

Step 5: Logical Order of Questions

  • Start with easy questions

  • Move to specific ones

  • Keep personal questions at the end

Think of it like warming up before exercise.

Step 6: Pilot Testing

Before final use, test the questionnaire on a small group.

This helps find:

  • Confusing questions

  • Missing options

  • Length issues

Then revise and improve.

7️⃣ Characteristics of Good Questionnaire

  • Clear objective

  • Simple language

  • Short and precise

  • Logical order

  • No double-barreled questions

(Double-barreled example:
“Are you satisfied with price and quality?”
Price and quality are two different things.)

8️⃣ Business Case Example

Suppose an online shopping company wants feedback.

They design a questionnaire using Likert scale:

Statements like:

  • The website is easy to use.

  • Delivery was timely.

  • Customer support was helpful.

Responses from 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree).

Then they calculate average score.

If average is below 3, they know improvement is needed.

That is practical use of Likert scale.

9️⃣ Comparison – Open vs Closed Questions

BasisOpen-endedClosed-ended
Answer TypeFree responseFixed options
AnalysisDifficultEasy
TimeMoreLess
DepthHighLimited

Most business research prefers closed-ended questions with Likert scale.

🔟 Advantages of Proper Questionnaire Design

  • Reliable data

  • Easy analysis

  • High response rate

  • Better decision-making

⚠️ Limitations

  • Poor design leads to wrong data

  • Respondent bias

  • Non-response problem

🏁 Final Takeaway

Attitudinal scales like Likert scale help convert feelings into numbers.

Questionnaire designing ensures those numbers are accurate and meaningful.

In simple words:

👉 Good questions = Good data
👉 Good data = Good decisions

❓ Question: Explain Pre-Experimental, Quasi-Experimental and True-Experimental Designs in detail.

✅ Answer:

1️⃣ Big Picture – Why Do We Need Experimental Designs?

Think of a doctor testing a new medicine.

If the doctor wants to know whether the medicine actually cures patients, he must compare:

  • Patients who took the medicine

  • Patients who did not

Only then can he say whether the medicine works.

This is called cause-and-effect testing.

In business research, we often want to know:

  • Does advertisement increase sales?

  • Does training improve performance?

  • Does discount attract customers?

To answer such questions, we use experimental research design.

Experimental designs are mainly of three types:

  1. Pre-Experimental

  2. Quasi-Experimental

  3. True-Experimental

Let’s understand each clearly.

2️⃣ Pre-Experimental Design

What is it?

Pre-experimental design is the simplest and weakest form of experimental design.

It studies the effect of one variable but does not properly control other factors.

Think of it like this:

You try a new study method and score higher marks.
But you don’t know if the marks improved because of the study method or because the exam was easy.

There is no comparison group.

Features:

  • No random selection

  • No proper control group

  • Low accuracy

  • Simple to conduct

Example:

A company gives sales training to employees and checks sales after training.

If sales increase, they assume training worked.

But maybe:

  • Market demand increased

  • Festival season came

  • Competitor prices increased

Since these factors were not controlled, results are weak.

Advantages:

  • Easy to conduct

  • Low cost

  • Quick results

Disadvantages:

  • Results are not reliable

  • Cannot strongly prove cause and effect

  • High risk of error

3️⃣ Quasi-Experimental Design

What is it?

Quasi-experimental design is stronger than pre-experimental but weaker than true-experimental.

It includes a comparison group, but there is no random assignment.

Think of it like comparing two existing classrooms:

  • One uses smart board

  • One uses blackboard

Students were already in those classes — not randomly assigned.

So comparison is possible, but control is limited.

Features:

  • Has control/comparison group

  • No random selection

  • Moderate reliability

Example:

A company introduces a new marketing strategy in Branch A but not in Branch B.

Then it compares sales performance.

Since branches were not randomly selected, results may be influenced by:

  • Location differences

  • Customer types

  • Staff performance

Advantages:

  • More practical in real-world situations

  • Better than pre-experimental

  • Moderate control

Disadvantages:

  • Still risk of bias

  • No full control over variables

4️⃣ True-Experimental Design

What is it?

True-experimental design is the strongest and most reliable experimental design.

It uses:

  • Random selection

  • Control group

  • Experimental group

  • Control over external variables

Think of it like a scientific lab test.

Random assignment ensures fairness.

Key Elements:

  1. Independent Variable (Cause)

  2. Dependent Variable (Effect)

  3. Control Group

  4. Experimental Group

  5. Random Assignment

Example:

A company randomly divides 100 employees into two groups:

  • Group A receives training

  • Group B does not

After one month, performance is compared.

Since groups were randomly selected, external factors are minimized.

This gives strong evidence of cause and effect.

Advantages:

  • High accuracy

  • Strong proof

  • Less bias

  • Reliable results

Disadvantages:

  • Expensive

  • Time-consuming

  • Sometimes difficult to implement in real business settings

5️⃣ Comparison Table

BasisPre-ExperimentalQuasi-ExperimentalTrue-Experimental
Random AssignmentNoNoYes
Control GroupNo/WeakYesYes
ReliabilityLowModerateHigh
CostLowMediumHigh
AccuracyWeakBetterStrong

6️⃣ Real Business Case Example

Suppose an online company wants to test a new website design.

Pre-Experimental:

They change the website and check sales after change.
No comparison group.

Weak conclusion.

Quasi-Experimental:

They launch new design in one city and keep old design in another city.
Compare sales.

Better, but cities may differ.

True-Experimental:

They randomly show new design to 50% of website visitors and old design to 50%.
Then compare purchases.

Strong and reliable result.

7️⃣ When to Use Which?

  • If budget is low → Pre-experimental

  • If real-world setting → Quasi-experimental

  • If high accuracy required → True-experimental

In business research, true-experimental is ideal, but sometimes not practical.

🏁 Final Takeaway

Experimental designs help us understand cause and effect.

  • Pre-experimental = Basic and weak

  • Quasi-experimental = Moderate

  • True-experimental = Strong and scientific

In simple words:

👉 More control = More accuracy
👉 Less control = Less reliability

That is the core idea behind experimental research designs.

1️⃣ Big Picture – What is Statistical Design?

Imagine you are cooking for 200 people.

But if you measure properly using calculations, the result will be perfect.

👉 Statistical design means planning research in such a way that statistical tools can be properly applied to analyze data.

It ensures:

  • Correct sample size

  • Proper grouping

  • Scientific comparison

  • Accurate analysis

Without statistical planning, even good data cannot give correct conclusions.

2️⃣ Why Statistical Design is Important

Think of it like building a bridge.

If the engineer does not calculate weight, pressure, and strength, the bridge may collapse.

In research:

  • Statistical design protects from wrong conclusions

  • Reduces error

  • Increases reliability

  • Helps test hypothesis scientifically

It makes research more scientific and less based on guessing.

3️⃣ Key Elements of Statistical Design

1. Randomization

Random selection of participants into groups.

Why?
To reduce bias and make groups similar.

Example: Randomly dividing employees into training and non-training groups.

2. Replication

Repeating the experiment multiple times.

Why?
To check consistency of results.

Example: Testing the same advertisement in multiple cities.

3. Control

Controlling unwanted variables.

Why?
To isolate the effect of the independent variable.

Example: Keeping price constant while testing advertisement impact.

4️⃣ Types of Statistical Experimental Designs

A. Completely Randomized Design (CRD)

  • Subjects randomly assigned

  • Simple design

  • Used when groups are homogeneous

Example: Randomly assigning fertilizers to plants.

B. Randomized Block Design (RBD)

  • Subjects grouped into blocks based on similarity

  • Then random assignment within each block

Example: Dividing students by grade level, then applying teaching methods.

C. Latin Square Design

  • Controls two external variables

  • Used when two blocking factors exist

Example: Testing product in different cities and seasons.

D. Factorial Design

  • Studies effect of two or more independent variables simultaneously

Example: Testing advertisement type AND discount rate together.

This saves time and provides deeper analysis.

5️⃣ Validity in Experimental Research

Now let’s understand validity in experiments.

Validity ensures that results are correct and meaningful.

There are mainly two types:

  1. Internal Validity

  2. External Validity

A. Internal Validity

Internal validity means the experiment truly measures cause and effect.

It answers:

👉 Did the independent variable really cause the change?

Threats to internal validity:

  • History effect (external events)

  • Maturation (natural changes)

  • Testing effect

  • Instrumentation change

Example:

If sales increase after advertisement, was it because of ad or because festival season started?

True-experimental designs have high internal validity.

B. External Validity

External validity means whether results can be generalized to other situations.

It answers:

👉 Can we apply these results elsewhere?

Example:

If training works in one branch, will it work in all branches?

Sometimes experiments done in controlled settings may not reflect real-world conditions.

TypeMeaningFocus
Internal ValidityTrue cause-effectAccuracy inside study
External ValidityGeneralizationApplicability outside study

Strong research tries to balance both.

7️⃣ Business Case Example

Suppose a company tests two pricing strategies.

Step 1: Randomization

Customers randomly shown price A or price B.

Step 2: Control

Same product quality and advertisement maintained.

Step 3: Replication

Test repeated in multiple cities.

Now statistical tools like ANOVA can compare results.

If price B increases sales significantly, company adopts it.

That is power of statistical design.

8️⃣ Advantages of Statistical Design

  • Scientific accuracy

  • Reduces bias

  • Allows complex analysis

  • Saves time in long run

  • Helps strong hypothesis testing

9️⃣ Limitations

  • Complex to design

  • Requires statistical knowledge

  • Expensive in large experiments

🏁 Final Takeaway

Statistical design makes research scientific.

It uses:

  • Randomization

  • Control

  • Replication

And ensures high validity.

In simple words:

👉 Good statistical planning = Reliable results
👉 Poor planning = Misleading conclusions

That is why statistical design is the backbone of experimental research.

Imagine you buy a thermometer to check your body temperature.

That is what validity means in research.

👉 Validity means the research tool measures what it is supposed to measure.

If validity is weak:

  • Results become misleading

  • Business decisions become risky

  • Research loses credibility

So validity is the heart of research instruments like questionnaires, tests, and surveys.

2️⃣ Why Validity is Important

Let’s say a company wants to measure customer satisfaction.

If the questionnaire accidentally measures brand awareness instead of satisfaction, the company may make wrong decisions.

So validity ensures:

  • Correct measurement

  • Accurate results

  • Reliable conclusions

  • Better decision-making

Without validity, research becomes guesswork.

3️⃣ Types of Validity

In your syllabus, three important types are mentioned:

4️⃣ Face Validity

What is Face Validity?

Face validity means the tool appears correct on the surface.

It is about first impression.

It answers:

👉 “Does this tool look like it measures what it claims to measure?”

It is the simplest type of validity.

Example:

If a survey claims to measure employee satisfaction and asks questions like:

  • Are you happy with your salary?

  • Are you satisfied with your work environment?

  • Do you feel valued at work?

Important Point:

Face validity is subjective.

Advantages:

  • Easy to check

  • Quick evaluation

Limitation:

  • Only surface-level checking

  • Not scientifically strong

5️⃣ Content Validity

What is Content Validity?

Content validity means the tool covers all important parts of the concept.

Think of it like an exam paper.

If the syllabus has 5 chapters but questions come only from 2 chapters, the exam lacks content validity.

Example:

If measuring employee performance, the questionnaire should include:

  • Technical skills

  • Communication

  • Productivity

  • Punctuality

If any major area is missing, content validity is weak.

  • Ensure complete coverage

Advantages:

  • Comprehensive measurement

  • Reduces missing information

Limitation:

  • Requires expert knowledge

  • May still not capture depth

This is the deepest and most important type.

Construct means an abstract idea like:

  • Motivation

  • Intelligence

  • Loyalty

  • Satisfaction

These cannot be directly seen or touched.

Construct validity checks:

👉 “Does the tool truly measure the concept?”

Example:

Suppose we want to measure motivation.

  • Desire to achieve goals

Advantages:

Limitation:

  • Requires statistical testing

  • Time-consuming
