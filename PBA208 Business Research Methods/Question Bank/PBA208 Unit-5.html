❓ Question: What is the difference between Parametric and Non-Parametric tests?

Answer:

A parametric test is a statistical test that assumes the data follows a normal distribution and has equal variance. It works with numerical data like marks, income, height, etc.

In other words, parametric tests need certain rules (assumptions) about the data before we use them.

Status: Unit 5 | Topic: Parametric vs Non-Parametric Tests | 15 Marks | Q 1/10

❓ Question: Explain Parametric and Non-Parametric Tests in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Start with the idea)

Imagine you are a manager trying to compare employee performance in three branches. You collect their sales numbers.

Now the big question is — which statistical test should you use?

That depends on one simple thing:

👉 Does your data follow certain rules (assumptions), or not?

This is where Parametric and Non-Parametric tests come in.

Think of it like this:

  • Parametric tests are like driving on a highway — smooth road, clear rules, faster results.

  • Non-parametric tests are like driving on a village road — flexible, fewer rules, works even when conditions are not perfect.

Let’s break this down properly.

🔹 1. What are Parametric Tests?

Parametric tests are statistical tests that assume certain things about the data.

The most important assumptions are:

  1. Data follows a normal distribution (bell-shaped curve).

  2. Data is measured on interval or ratio scale (like income, weight, marks).

  3. Variance is equal across groups.

  4. Sample size is reasonably large.

If these conditions are satisfied, parametric tests give more accurate and powerful results.

📌 Common Parametric Tests

  • t-test (compare two means)

  • ANOVA (compare more than two means)

  • Pearson correlation

  • Regression analysis

  • Z-test

🎯 Why Use Parametric Tests?

Because they:

  • Give precise results

  • Detect small differences

  • Have high statistical power

But only when assumptions are satisfied.

🧠 Simple Example

Suppose a company wants to compare the average salary of employees in two departments.

Salary is numerical and usually follows a normal distribution.

So, a t-test (parametric test) is suitable.

🔹 2. What are Non-Parametric Tests?

Non-parametric tests are used when data does not meet the assumptions required for parametric tests.

They:

  • Do not require normal distribution

  • Can work with small samples

  • Can handle ordinal data (rankings)

  • Can work with categorical data

Think of them as more flexible but slightly less powerful tools.

📌 Common Non-Parametric Tests

  • Mann–Whitney U Test

  • Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test

  • Kruskal–Wallis Test

  • Chi-square Test

  • Run Test

  • Spearman Rank Correlation

🎯 Why Use Non-Parametric Tests?

Because:

  • Data is not normal

  • Sample size is small

  • Data is in ranks (1st, 2nd, 3rd)

  • Data is categorical (Yes/No, Male/Female)

🧠 Simple Example

A company surveys customers and asks them to rate service as:

  • Poor

  • Average

  • Good

  • Excellent

This is ranking (ordinal data), not numerical measurement.

So, a non-parametric test like Kruskal–Wallis is used.

🔹 3. Key Differences (Easy Comparison Table)

BasisParametric TestNon-Parametric Test
AssumptionsRequires normal distributionNo strict assumptions
Data TypeInterval/RatioOrdinal/Categorical
Sample SizeLarge preferredSmall also acceptable
PowerMore powerfulLess powerful

🔹 4. When to Choose Which?

Use Parametric Tests When:

✔ Data is normally distributed
✔ Sample size is large
✔ Variance is equal
✔ Data is numerical

Use Non-Parametric Tests When:

✔ Data is skewed
✔ Sample size is small
✔ Data is ranking or categories
✔ Assumptions are violated

🔹 5. Real Business Case Study

📊 Case: Retail Store Performance Analysis

A retail chain wants to compare sales performance across 4 cities.

Step 1: They check data distribution.

  • If sales numbers follow normal distribution → Use ANOVA

  • If sales numbers are highly skewed → Use Kruskal-Wallis Test

Now suppose they conduct a customer satisfaction survey using 1 to 5 rating scale.

That is ordinal data.

So instead of Pearson correlation, they use Spearman rank correlation.

🔹 6. Advantages and Disadvantages

✅ Advantages of Parametric Tests

  • More accurate

  • More powerful

  • Gives detailed conclusions

❌ Disadvantages of Parametric Tests

  • Strict assumptions

  • Not suitable for small samples

✅ Advantages of Non-Parametric Tests

  • Flexible

  • Works with small samples

  • Suitable for ranking data

❌ Disadvantages of Non-Parametric Tests

  • Less powerful

  • May lose some detailed information

🔹 7. Why Does This Matter in Exams?

Examiners want to know:

  • Do you understand assumptions?

  • Can you choose correct test?

  • Can you compare both clearly?

If you explain with examples and table comparison, you score full 15 marks easily.

🏁 Final Takeaway

Parametric tests are powerful tools used when data follows strict assumptions like normal distribution. Non-parametric tests are flexible alternatives used when those assumptions are not met. The choice depends on the type and nature of data.

Status: Unit 5 | Topic: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) | 15 Marks | Q 2/10

❓ Question: Explain ANOVA in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Start with a Simple Idea)

Imagine you are a manager who introduced three different training programs for employees.

After one month, you check their sales performance.

Now your question is:

👉 Which training program is best?
👉 Are the differences real or just luck?

If you compare two groups, you use a t-test.

But here you have three or more groups.

This is where ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) comes in.

Think of ANOVA like a referee in a tournament. It checks whether the teams are actually different or just appear different because of chance.

🔹 1. What is ANOVA?

ANOVA stands for Analysis of Variance.

It is a statistical technique used to test whether the means of three or more groups are significantly different from each other.

In simple words:

ANOVA checks whether the difference between group averages is real or accidental.

🔹 2. Why Do We Need ANOVA?

You may ask:

“Why not just do multiple t-tests?”

Good question.

If you compare:

  • Group A vs B

  • Group A vs C

  • Group B vs C

Doing multiple t-tests increases the chance of making mistakes (Type I error).

ANOVA solves this by comparing all groups in one single test.

So it:
✔ Saves time
✔ Reduces error
✔ Gives reliable conclusion

🔹 3. Basic Idea Behind ANOVA

Here’s the simple logic.

ANOVA compares two types of variation:

1️⃣ Between-group variation
Difference between the averages of groups.

2️⃣ Within-group variation
Difference inside each group (natural differences among individuals).

💡 Think of it Like This

Imagine 3 classrooms.

  • Each class has average marks.

  • Students inside each class also differ.

ANOVA checks:

👉 Is the difference between class averages bigger than the difference inside each class?

If yes → Real difference
If no → Just random variation

🔹 4. Types of ANOVA

1️⃣ One-Way ANOVA

Used when:

  • One independent variable

  • Three or more groups

Example:
Comparing sales of employees trained under 3 methods.

2️⃣ Two-Way ANOVA

Used when:

  • Two independent variables

Example:
Comparing sales based on:

  • Training method

  • Experience level

3️⃣ Repeated Measures ANOVA

Used when:

  • Same group measured multiple times

Example:
Measuring employee performance before training, after 1 month, after 3 months.

🔹 5. Assumptions of ANOVA

ANOVA works properly only if:

✔ Data follows normal distribution
✔ Groups are independent
✔ Variance is equal (homogeneity of variance)
✔ Data is interval or ratio scale

If assumptions are violated, we use Kruskal-Wallis test (non-parametric alternative).

🔹 6. ANOVA Table (Basic Structure)

Source of VariationSum of SquaresDegrees of FreedomMean SquareF Value

Don’t panic about formulas.

The key idea:

👉 F value = Variation between groups ÷ Variation within groups

If F is large → Significant difference
If F is small → No real difference

🔹 7. Steps in ANOVA

  1. State Hypothesis

    • H₀: All group means are equal

    • H₁: At least one mean is different

  2. Calculate group means

  3. Compute F value

  4. Compare with critical value

  5. Draw conclusion

🔹 8. Business Case Study

📊 Case: Marketing Strategy Comparison

A company tests 4 advertisement strategies.

After 3 months, sales results are:

  • Strategy A → ₹10 lakh

  • Strategy B → ₹12 lakh

  • Strategy C → ₹11 lakh

  • Strategy D → ₹18 lakh

ANOVA is used to check:

👉 Is Strategy D really better?
👉 Or is this just market fluctuation?

Suppose ANOVA gives F value significant.

Conclusion:
At least one strategy differs.

Then company does Post Hoc Test (like Tukey test) to find which one.

🔹 9. Advantages of ANOVA

✔ Can compare more than two groups
✔ Reduces Type I error
✔ Powerful statistical method
✔ Widely used in research

🔹 10. Limitations of ANOVA

❌ Only tells that difference exists, not where
❌ Requires normal distribution
❌ Sensitive to outliers

🔹 11. When to Use ANOVA in Exams?

Use ANOVA when:

✔ Comparing 3 or more means
✔ Research involves multiple treatments
✔ Experimental design is involved

🏁 Final Takeaway

ANOVA is a statistical tool used to compare the means of three or more groups by analyzing variation within and between groups. It helps determine whether differences are real or due to chance and is widely used in business, marketing, and experimental research.

❓ Question: Explain Correlation and Regression in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Understand the Core Idea First)

Imagine you are running a business.

You notice something interesting:

  • When advertising increases, sales also increase.

  • When employee training increases, productivity improves.

  • When prices increase too much, demand falls.

Now the big question is:

👉 Are these things connected?
👉 If yes, how strong is the connection?
👉 Can we predict one using the other?

This is where Correlation and Regression come in.

Think of it this way:

  • Correlation tells you whether two things move together.

  • Regression tells you how much one thing changes when the other changes.

Correlation shows relationship.
Regression shows prediction.

🔹 1. What is Correlation?

Correlation measures the degree and direction of relationship between two variables.

In simple words:

It tells us whether two variables are connected and how strongly they are connected.

📌 Types of Correlation

1️⃣ Positive Correlation

When both variables move in the same direction.

Example:
More advertising → More sales.

2️⃣ Negative Correlation

When one increases, the other decreases.

Example:
Higher price → Lower demand.

3️⃣ Zero Correlation

No relationship between variables.

Example:
Shoe size and intelligence level.

🔹 2. Correlation Coefficient (r)

Correlation is measured using a value called r.

It ranges from:

  • +1 → Perfect positive relationship

  • 0 → No relationship

  • -1 → Perfect negative relationship

💡 Easy Interpretation Table

Value of rMeaning
+0.80 to +1Strong positive
+0.30 to +0.79Moderate positive
0No relationship
-0.30 to -0.79Moderate negative
-0.80 to -1Strong negative

🔹 3. Limitations of Correlation

Important point for exams:

👉 Correlation does NOT mean causation.

Just because two things move together doesn’t mean one causes the other.

Example:
Ice cream sales and drowning cases both increase in summer.
That doesn’t mean ice cream causes drowning.

🔹 4. What is Regression?

Now comes the powerful part.

Regression not only shows relationship — it helps predict.

In simple words:

Regression tells how much change happens in one variable when the other changes.

It is used when:

  • One variable depends on another.

  • We want forecasting.

🔹 5. Types of Regression

1️⃣ Simple Regression

One independent variable, one dependent variable.

Example:
Sales (dependent) based on advertising (independent).

2️⃣ Multiple Regression

More than one independent variable.

Example:
Sales based on:

  • Advertising

  • Price

  • Income level

  • Season

🔹 6. Regression Equation

The basic formula:

Y = a + bX

Where:

  • Y = Dependent variable

  • X = Independent variable

  • a = Intercept

  • b = Slope

💡 Meaning of b (Slope)

Slope tells:

👉 If X increases by 1 unit, how much does Y increase?

Example:

If b = 5
It means if advertising increases by ₹1, sales increase by ₹5.

  • Easy to calculate

  • Helps in decision making

  • Useful for forecasting

✔ Sales forecasting
✔ Demand prediction
✔ Cost estimation
✔ Risk analysis

Most business decisions today are based on regression models.

🏁 Final Takeaway

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the relationship between two variables, while regression goes a step further by predicting the value of one variable based on another. Both are powerful tools in business research for analysis, forecasting, and strategic planning.

❓ Question: Explain the Chi-Square Test in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Start with a Simple Situation)

Imagine you are a marketing manager.

You launch two versions of a product:

  • Product A

  • Product B

After one month, you collect data:

  • Male customers preferred A more.

  • Female customers preferred B more.

Now your question is:

👉 Is product preference related to gender?
👉 Or is this difference just by chance?

Since both variables are categories (Male/Female, A/B), we cannot use t-test or ANOVA.

This is where the Chi-Square Test comes in.

Think of Chi-square as a tool that checks:

👉 Are two categorical variables connected?

🔹 1. What is the Chi-Square Test?

The Chi-Square Test (χ² test) is a non-parametric statistical test used to examine whether there is a significant association between two categorical variables.

In simple words:

It checks whether the difference between expected and observed results is real or random.

🔹 2. Types of Chi-Square Tests

There are mainly two types:

1️⃣ Chi-Square Test of Independence

Used to check whether two categorical variables are related.

Example:
Is customer preference related to gender?

2️⃣ Chi-Square Test of Goodness of Fit

Used to check whether observed data fits a theoretical distribution.

Example:
Do customer visits follow expected weekly pattern?

🔹 3. Basic Concept Behind Chi-Square

Chi-square compares:

Observed Frequency (what actually happened)

with

Expected Frequency (what should have happened if no relationship exists)

If the difference is large → relationship exists
If the difference is small → no relationship

💡 Simple Analogy

Imagine tossing a coin 100 times.

Expected:
50 Heads, 50 Tails

Observed:
80 Heads, 20 Tails

Chi-square checks whether this difference is due to chance or something unusual.

🔹 4. Formula of Chi-Square

χ² = Σ (O - E)² / E

Where:

O = Observed frequency
E = Expected frequency

Don’t panic about formula.

Just remember:

It measures how far actual results are from expected results.

🔹 5. Steps in Chi-Square Test

  1. State Hypothesis

    • H₀: No relationship between variables

    • H₁: Relationship exists

  2. Create contingency table

  3. Calculate expected frequencies

  4. Compute χ² value

  5. Compare with critical value

  6. Draw conclusion

🔹 6. Assumptions of Chi-Square Test

✔ Data must be categorical
✔ Observations must be independent
✔ Expected frequency should not be less than 5
✔ Sample size should be reasonably large

If expected values are too small, test may not be reliable.

🔹 7. Business Case Study

📊 Case: Online Shopping Preference

A company wants to check whether age group affects online shopping preference.

Data collected:

18–3012030
31–508070

Chi-square test is applied.

If χ² value is significant:

Conclusion:
Age group and online shopping preference are related.

If not significant:

Conclusion:
No association exists.

🔹 8. Advantages of Chi-Square Test

✔ Simple to calculate
✔ No strict distribution assumptions
✔ Useful for categorical data
✔ Widely used in business and social research

🔹 9. Limitations

❌ Cannot measure strength of relationship
❌ Requires large sample size
❌ Not suitable for small expected frequencies
❌ Only tells association, not causation

🔹 10. When to Use Chi-Square?

Use when:

✔ Data is categorical
✔ Studying association between two attributes
✔ Testing goodness of fit
✔ Conducting survey-based research

Common business uses:

  • Market segmentation

  • Consumer preference analysis

  • Brand loyalty studies

  • HR studies (gender vs job role)

🔹 11. Practical Application in Business

Companies use Chi-square to:

✔ Check if advertisement success differs by region
✔ Study relationship between education level and income category
✔ Analyze buying behavior patterns

It helps managers take data-driven decisions.

🏁 Final Takeaway

The Chi-Square Test is a non-parametric statistical method used to examine whether there is a significant association between categorical variables by comparing observed and expected frequencies. It is widely used in business research, marketing studies, and survey analysis to test relationships between attributes.

❓ Question: Explain Non-Parametric Tests for Normality in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Why This Topic Matters)

Before using powerful tests like t-test or ANOVA, one important question must be answered:

👉 Is my data normally distributed?

Many statistical tests assume that data follows a normal distribution (bell-shaped curve).

If this assumption is wrong, the results can be misleading.

So first, we test for normality.

If data is not normal, we use non-parametric tests instead of parametric ones.

Think of it like checking road conditions before driving fast. If the road is smooth (normal), you drive fast (parametric tests). If the road is rough (not normal), you drive carefully (non-parametric tests).

🔹 1. What is Normal Distribution?

A normal distribution:

✔ Is bell-shaped
✔ Is symmetrical
✔ Mean = Median = Mode
✔ Most values lie near the center

Many real-life variables like height, weight, IQ scores often follow normal distribution.

🔹 2. Why Test for Normality?

Because parametric tests like:

  • t-test

  • ANOVA

  • Regression

require data to be normally distributed.

If data is not normal:

  • Results may be biased

  • Type I or Type II errors may increase

  • Conclusions may be wrong

So checking normality is an important first step.

🔹 3. What are Non-Parametric Tests for Normality?

These are statistical tests used to determine whether a dataset significantly differs from a normal distribution.

They do not assume normality. Instead, they check if the sample data matches the expected pattern of normal distribution.

🔹 4. Common Tests for Normality

1️⃣ Shapiro-Wilk Test

  • Most powerful test for small samples

  • Suitable when sample size < 50

  • Commonly used in research

If p-value < 0.05 → Data is not normal
If p-value > 0.05 → Data is normal

2️⃣ Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test

  • Used for larger samples

  • Compares sample distribution with theoretical normal distribution

3️⃣ Anderson-Darling Test

  • More sensitive in detecting deviations

  • Focuses more on tails of distribution

4️⃣ Jarque-Bera Test

  • Based on skewness and kurtosis

  • Used in finance and economics

🔹 5. Graphical Methods to Check Normality

Non-parametric tests for normality are statistical tools used to determine whether data follows a normal distribution. Since many parametric tests require normality, these tests help researchers choose the correct method of analysis and avoid misleading conclusions.

❓ Question: Explain the Run Test in detail.

🌍 The Big Picture (Start with a Simple Situation)

Imagine you are observing daily sales of a store.

You record whether sales were:

  • High (H)

  • Low (L)

The sequence looks like this:

H H H L L H L H H L

Now your question is:

👉 Is this pattern random?
👉 Or is there some hidden trend?

This is where the Run Test helps.

Think of the Run Test as a tool that checks whether a sequence of data is random or shows a pattern.

🔹 1. What is a Run Test?

The Run Test is a non-parametric statistical test used to determine whether a sequence of observations is occurring randomly.

In simple words:

It checks whether the order of data values is random or not.

It is also called the Wald-Wolfowitz Run Test.

🔹 2. What is a “Run”?

A run is a sequence of similar elements.

For example:

Sequence:
H H L L H L L H

Runs are:
(H H) → Run 1
(L L) → Run 2
(H) → Run 3
(L L) → Run 4
(H) → Run 5

So total runs = 5

A change from H to L or L to H creates a new run.

🔹 3. Purpose of Run Test

The run test helps to:

✔ Check randomness
✔ Detect patterns
✔ Identify trends
✔ Analyze time series data

It is useful when data does not follow normal distribution.

🔹 4. Hypothesis in Run Test

H₀ (Null Hypothesis):
Data is random.

H₁ (Alternative Hypothesis):
Data is not random.

🔹 5. Steps in Run Test

  1. Arrange data in sequence

  2. Identify number of runs

  3. Count number of positive and negative signs

  4. Calculate expected runs

  5. Compute test statistic (Z value)

  6. Compare with critical value

  7. Draw conclusion

🔹 6. Formula (Basic Idea)

Expected number of runs:

E(R) = (2n₁n₂ / n₁ + n₂) + 1

Where:

n₁ = number of positive observations
n₂ = number of negative observations

Then Z value is calculated.

If Z value is large → Not random
If Z value is small → Random

You don’t need to memorize full formula for theory exam, but understanding logic is important.

🔹 7. Example for Clear Understanding

📊 Case: Stock Market Analysis

A financial analyst observes stock movement for 15 days:

Up, Up, Down, Down, Up, Down, Down, Up, Up, Down, Up, Down, Down, Up, Down

He wants to check whether stock movement is random or shows trend.

He applies run test.

If result shows too few runs:
Trend exists.

If too many runs:
Possible instability.

If normal runs:
Random movement.

🔹 8. Applications in Business

✔ Checking randomness in sales data
✔ Testing quality control processes
✔ Analyzing stock price movement
✔ Studying production defects
✔ Checking marketing campaign response patterns

It is widely used in time-series analysis.

🔹 9. Advantages of Run Test

✔ Simple to understand
✔ No distribution assumptions
✔ Useful for small samples
✔ Suitable for ordinal data

🔹 10. Limitations

❌ Only checks randomness
❌ Does not measure strength of trend
❌ Sensitive to extreme values

It only tells whether sequence is random — not why.

🔹 11. When to Use Run Test?

Use when:

✔ Data is arranged in sequence
✔ You want to test randomness
✔ Parametric assumptions are not satisfied
✔ Dealing with quality control or time-based data

🏁 Final Takeaway

The Run Test is a non-parametric statistical method used to determine whether a sequence of observations occurs randomly. It counts the number of runs in data and compares it with expected runs to identify whether a pattern or trend exists. It is widely used in business research, quality control, and time-series analysis.

🔹 1. What is Factor Analysis?

Factor Analysis is a multivariate statistical technique used to reduce a large number of variables into a smaller number of underlying factors.

In simple words:

It identifies hidden patterns and groups related variables into factors.

🔹 2. Why Do We Need Factor Analysis?

Because in real business research:

Factor analysis simplifies the data and makes it manageable.

🔹 3. Basic Concept Behind Factor Analysis

Example:

  • Clean store

  • Polite staff

  • Quick billing

🔹 4. Types of Factor Analysis

  • You already have a theory.

Testing whether customer satisfaction truly consists of 3 factors.

🔹 5. Steps in Factor Analysis

  6. Interpret factors

🔹 1. What is Discriminant Analysis?

Discriminant Analysis is a statistical technique used to classify observations into predefined groups based on independent variables.

In simple words:

It helps identify which variables differentiate between two or more groups.

🔹 2. Purpose of Discriminant Analysis

✔ Classify individuals into categories
✔ Predict group membership
✔ Identify important discriminating variables

🔹 3. When is it Used?

It is used when:

🔹 4. Basic Concept

Discriminant Analysis creates a mathematical function:

D = a + b₁X₁ + b₂X₂ + b₃X₃ + … + bₙXₙ

Where:

  • D = Discriminant score

  • X₁, X₂ = Independent variables

  • b₁, b₂ = Coefficients

This function separates groups as clearly as possible.

🔹 5. Types of Discriminant Analysis

Low, Medium, High income groups.

  6. Evaluate classification accuracy
