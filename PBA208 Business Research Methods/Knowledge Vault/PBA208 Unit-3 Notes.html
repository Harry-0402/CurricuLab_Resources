1. Meaning and Concept of Sampling

1.1 Introduction

In many research studies‚Äîespecially in business‚Äîit is impractical, costly, and time-consuming to study every unit of a population. Instead of examining the entire population, researchers select a small but representative portion, known as a sample.

The process of selecting this representative portion is called sampling, and the plan used for selection is known as the sampling design.

1.2 Meaning of Sampling

Sampling refers to the process of selecting a subset of elements from a large population in such a way that the selected sample represents the characteristics of the entire population.

Definitions

  • C. R. Kothari:

    ‚ÄúSampling is the selection of some part of an aggregate or totality on the basis of which a judgment or inference about the aggregate or totality is made.‚Äù

Key Terms Explained

TermMeaning
PopulationEntire group under study
SampleSelected subset of population
Sampling UnitIndividual element selected
Sampling FrameList of population elements

Example (Business Context)

A company wants to study customer satisfaction of 10,000 customers. Surveying all customers is difficult, so it selects 500 customers as a sample.

2. Importance of Sampling

2.1 Introduction

Sampling plays a crucial role in business research because it allows researchers to obtain reliable results with limited resources.

2.2 Importance of Sampling in Research

2.2.1 Saves Time

  • Studying a sample takes less time

  • Enables faster decision-making

2.2.2 Reduces Cost

  • Lower expenses on:

    • Data collection

    • Fieldwork

    • Data processing

2.2.3 Practical Feasibility

  • Census is often impossible in large populations

  • Sampling makes research feasible

2.2.4 Accuracy and Reliability

  • Proper sampling can produce results as accurate as a census

  • Better supervision reduces errors

2.2.5 Scientific Decision-Making

  • Sampling supports statistical inference

  • Helps generalize findings to the population

Summary of Importance of Sampling

Sampling ensures:

  • Economy

  • Speed

  • Accuracy

  • Practicality

Hence, it is indispensable in business research.

3. Census vs Sampling

3.1 Census Method

A census involves collecting data from every unit of the population.

Advantages of Census Method

  • Complete and detailed information

  • High accuracy (if no errors occur)

Limitations of Census Method

  • Very expensive

  • Time-consuming

  • Not feasible for large populations

3.2 Sampling Method

Sampling involves collecting data from only a part of the population.

Advantages of Sampling Method

  • Economical

  • Faster results

  • Suitable for large populations

Limitations of Sampling Method

  • Possibility of sampling errors

  • Requires careful planning

Summary of Census vs Sampling

  • Census is ideal but often impractical

  • Sampling is widely used in business and social research

4. Sampling Design

4.1 Meaning of Sampling Design

A sampling design is the definite plan for obtaining a sample from a given population.

It specifies:

  • Sampling method

  • Sample size

  • Selection procedure

4.2 Objectives of Sampling Design

  • To obtain a representative sample

  • To reduce sampling errors

  • To ensure economy and efficiency

4.3 Factors Affecting Sampling Design

4.3.1 Nature of Population

  • Homogeneous or heterogeneous population

4.3.2 Type of Research

  • Exploratory, descriptive, or experimental

4.3.3 Availability of Sampling Frame

  • Accurate list of population elements

4.3.4 Time and Cost Constraints

  • Budget and time available for research

Summary of Sampling Design

  • Sampling design determines quality of research results

  • Poor design leads to misleading conclusions

5. Sampling Process

5.1 Introduction

Sampling is not a random or casual activity. It follows a systematic and well-defined process to ensure that the selected sample truly represents the population.

A proper sampling process reduces bias and errors, thereby improving the reliability of research findings.

5.2 Steps in the Sampling Process

Step 1: Define the Population

  • Identify the entire group relevant to the research problem

  • Population may be:

    • Finite or infinite

    • Homogeneous or heterogeneous

Example:
All customers who purchased Product X in the last one year.

Step 2: Identify the Sampling Frame

  • A sampling frame is a list of all population elements

  • Examples:

    • Customer database

    • Voter list

    • Employee register

Note:
An inaccurate sampling frame leads to sampling error.

Step 3: Choose the Sampling Unit

  • A sampling unit is the basic unit selected from the population

  • It may be:

    • Individual

    • Household

    • Organization

Step 4: Select the Sampling Technique

  • Decide whether to use:

    • Probability sampling

    • Non-probability sampling

This decision depends on:

  • Nature of research

  • Availability of population list

  • Time and cost constraints

Step 5: Determine Sample Size

  • Sample size should be:

    • Adequate

    • Representative

    • Economically feasible

(Sample size determination will be studied in detail in Part 4.)

Step 6: Execute the Sampling Plan

  • Actual selection of sample units

  • Requires:

    • Proper supervision

    • Adherence to design

6. Types of Sampling Techniques

6.1 Introduction

Sampling techniques are broadly classified into two categories:

  1. Probability Sampling

  2. Non-Probability Sampling

In this part, we will focus on Probability Sampling Techniques.

7. Probability Sampling

7.1 Meaning

In probability sampling:

  • Each unit in the population has a known and equal chance of being selected

  • Selection is based on randomization

7.2 Characteristics of Probability Sampling

  • Scientific and objective

  • Allows estimation of sampling error

  • Results can be generalized to the population

7.3 Types of Probability Sampling Techniques

7.3.1 Simple Random Sampling

Meaning

Every unit of the population has:

  • An equal chance of selection

  • Selection is purely random

Methods of Simple Random Sampling

  1. Lottery Method

  2. Random Number Table

  3. Computer-generated random numbers

Example

From 1,000 employees, 100 are selected randomly using a random number generator.

Advantages

  • Simple and unbiased

  • Easy to understand

Limitations

  • Requires complete population list

  • Not suitable for heterogeneous populations

7.3.2 Stratified Random Sampling

Meaning

The population is divided into homogeneous sub-groups (strata), and random samples are drawn from each stratum.

Steps in Stratified Sampling

  1. Divide population into strata

  2. Select samples from each stratum randomly

Example

Employees divided into:

  • Managers

  • Supervisors

  • Workers

Samples drawn from each group.

Advantages

  • Better representation

  • Higher accuracy

Limitations

  • Requires detailed population information

  • More complex than simple random sampling

Comparison Table: Simple Random vs Stratified Sampling

BasisSimple RandomStratified
PopulationHomogeneousHeterogeneous
AccuracyModerateHigh
ComplexitySimpleComplex

Summary of Probability Sampling

  • Probability sampling is the most scientific method

  • Suitable for descriptive and experimental research

  • Provides reliable and generalizable results

7.3.3 Systematic Sampling

7.3.3.1 Meaning

Systematic sampling is a probability sampling technique in which:

  • Only the first unit is selected randomly

  • The remaining units are selected at fixed intervals

This interval is known as the sampling interval.

Where:

  • N = Population size

  • n = Sample size

Example

Population = 1,000 employees
Sample size = 100

k=1000100=10k = \frac{1000}{100} = 10k=1001000‚Äã=10

‚Üí Select every 10th employee after a random start.

Advantages of Systematic Sampling

  • Simple and convenient

  • Time-saving

  • Ensures even spread of sample

Limitations of Systematic Sampling

  • If population has hidden patterns, results may be biased

  • Less random than simple random sampling

Summary of Systematic Sampling

  • Combines simplicity with probability principles

  • Suitable when population list is available

7.3.4 Cluster Sampling

7.3.4.1 Meaning

In cluster sampling, the population is divided into clusters (groups), and:

  • Entire clusters are selected randomly

  • All elements within selected clusters are studied

Clusters are usually heterogeneous, unlike strata.

Example

A company studies retail outlets across India:

  • Cities are treated as clusters

  • Randomly selected cities are studied fully

Advantages of Cluster Sampling

  • Economical for large geographical areas

  • Reduces travel and administrative costs

Limitations of Cluster Sampling

  • Lower accuracy compared to stratified sampling

  • High sampling error if clusters are not representative

Difference Between Stratified and Cluster Sampling

BasisStratifiedCluster
Population divisionHomogeneous strataHeterogeneous clusters
Sample selectionFrom each stratumEntire clusters
AccuracyHighModerate

Summary of Cluster Sampling

  • Useful for large and dispersed populations

  • Widely used in marketing and social research

8. Non-Probability Sampling

8.1 Introduction

In many practical situations:

  • Population list is unavailable

  • Time and cost constraints exist

In such cases, non-probability sampling is used.

8.2 Meaning of Non-Probability Sampling

In non-probability sampling:

  • The chance of selection of units is unknown

  • Selection depends on researcher judgment or convenience

8.3 Characteristics of Non-Probability Sampling

  • Non-random selection

  • Quick and economical

  • Does not permit statistical generalization

8.4 Types of Non-Probability Sampling

8.4.1 Convenience Sampling

Meaning

Sample is selected based on:

  • Ease of access

  • Convenience of the researcher

Example

Surveying customers who visit a store on a particular day.

Advantages

  • Very easy and quick

  • Low cost

Limitations

  • Highly biased

  • Poor representativeness

8.4.2 Judgment Sampling

Meaning

Sample is selected based on the researcher‚Äôs judgment about who will provide the best information.

Example

Interviewing senior managers for a strategic study.

Advantages

  • Useful for expert opinions

  • Suitable for exploratory research

Limitations

  • Subjective

  • Results depend on researcher‚Äôs competence

8.4.3 Quota Sampling

Meaning

Population is divided into groups, and:

  • Fixed quotas are assigned

  • Samples are selected conveniently within each quota

Example

Interviewing:

  • 50 male customers

  • 50 female customers

Advantages

  • Ensures representation of key groups

  • Faster than stratified sampling

Limitations

  • Non-random selection

  • Possibility of interviewer bias

Summary of Non-Probability Sampling

  • Useful when probability sampling is not feasible

  • Common in exploratory and qualitative studies

  • Limited generalizability

9. Sample Size Determination

9.1 Introduction

After selecting the sampling technique, the next crucial decision is how many units should be selected. This decision is known as sample size determination.

A sample that is too small may give inaccurate results, while an excessively large sample may waste time, money, and effort. Therefore, determining an optimum sample size is essential in business research.

9.2 Meaning of Sample Size

Sample size refers to the number of units included in a sample.

Key Question

How large should the sample be so that it adequately represents the population?

9.3 Factors Affecting Sample Size

9.3.1 Nature of Population

  • Homogeneous population ‚Üí Smaller sample

  • Heterogeneous population ‚Üí Larger sample

9.3.2 Type of Research

  • Exploratory research ‚Üí Small sample

  • Descriptive/Experimental research ‚Üí Large sample

9.3.3 Sampling Technique Used

  • Probability sampling ‚Üí Smaller sample may suffice

  • Non-probability sampling ‚Üí Larger sample needed

9.3.4 Degree of Accuracy Required

  • Higher accuracy ‚Üí Larger sample

9.3.5 Time and Cost Constraints

  • Limited resources restrict sample size

9.3.6 Variability in the Population

  • More variability ‚Üí Larger sample size required

Summary of Factors Affecting Sample Size

FactorEffect on Sample Size
Population heterogeneityIncreases size
Accuracy requirementIncreases size
Budget constraintsDecreases size

9.4 Methods of Determining Sample Size

9.4.1 Census Method

  • Used when population is small

  • All units are studied

9.4.2 Rule of Thumb Method

  • Based on experience

  • Common in business research

Example:

  • 5%‚Äì10% of population

9.4.3 Statistical Formula Method (Conceptual)

Uses:

  • Confidence level

  • Standard deviation

  • Margin of error

(Detailed formulas will be covered in statistics-related units.)

9.5 Problems in Sample Size Determination

  • Lack of population data

  • Budget limitations

  • Time constraints

  • Non-response issues

Summary of Sample Size Determination

  • Sample size directly affects accuracy of results

  • Optimum size balances precision and economy

  • No single rule applies to all research studies
