1. Meaning and Definition of Research

1.1 Introduction

In business, managers constantly face problems such as declining sales, changing consumer preferences, employee dissatisfaction, or uncertainty in market demand. Making decisions based on intuition alone can be risky. Research provides a systematic, scientific, and logical way to understand such problems and arrive at reliable solutions.

Research is not just ‚Äúcollecting information.‚Äù It is a planned and structured investigation aimed at discovering new facts, verifying existing knowledge, or solving practical problems. In business studies, research forms the backbone of sound decision-making.

1.2 Meaning of Research

The word research is derived from:

  • ‚ÄúRe‚Äù ‚Üí again

  • ‚ÄúSearch‚Äù ‚Üí to look for

Thus, research literally means ‚Äúa repeated search for knowledge.‚Äù

In an academic and business sense, research refers to a careful inquiry or examination to discover new information or to establish facts and principles.

1.3 Definitions of Research

Several scholars have defined research from different perspectives:

  • C. R. Kothari:

    ‚ÄúResearch is a scientific and systematic search for pertinent information on a specific topic.‚Äù

  • Redman and Mory:

    ‚ÄúResearch is a systematized effort to gain new knowledge.‚Äù

  • Clifford Woody:

    ‚ÄúResearch comprises defining and redefining problems, formulating hypotheses, collecting, organizing, and evaluating data, making deductions, and reaching conclusions.‚Äù

1.4 Key Characteristics of Research

From the above definitions, we can identify the essential features of research:

  • Systematic ‚Äì follows a definite procedure

  • Scientific ‚Äì uses logical reasoning and evidence

  • Objective ‚Äì free from bias and personal opinions

  • Empirical ‚Äì based on observation and real data

  • Replicable ‚Äì results can be verified by others

2. Objectives of Research

2.1 Introduction

Every research activity is conducted with a clear purpose. Without objectives, research becomes directionless and inefficient. Objectives define what the researcher wants to achieve through the study.

2.2 Primary Objectives of Research

Research objectives can be broadly classified into the following categories:

2.2.1 To Gain Familiarity with a Phenomenon (Exploratory Objective)

  • Used when the problem is new or not clearly defined

  • Helps in understanding the nature of the problem

  • Common in market research and consumer behavior studies

Example:
A company exploring why customers are switching to online shopping platforms.

2.2.2 To Accurately Describe Characteristics (Descriptive Objective)

  • Focuses on describing features of a population or situation

  • Uses surveys and observational methods

Example:
Studying age, income, and spending patterns of mobile phone users.

2.2.3 To Determine Frequency of Occurrence

  • Helps in measuring how often something happens

Example:
How frequently customers visit a retail store in a month.

2.2.4 To Test Hypotheses (Analytical Objective)

  • Used to establish cause-and-effect relationships

  • Involves statistical testing

Example:
Testing whether advertising expenditure significantly affects sales.

2.2.5 To Discover New Facts or Verify Existing Knowledge

  • Research may confirm earlier findings or challenge them

  • Essential for academic and policy-related studies

2.3 Business-Oriented Objectives of Research

In business management, research objectives often include:

  • Improving decision-making quality

  • Reducing risk and uncertainty

  • Identifying market opportunities

  • Evaluating employee performance

  • Measuring customer satisfaction

3. Types of Research

3.1 Introduction

Research can be classified in several ways depending on:

  • Purpose

  • Nature of data

  • Method used

  • Application of results

Understanding these types helps researchers select the most suitable approach for their study.

3.2 Basic (Fundamental) and Applied Research

3.2.1 Basic (Fundamental) Research

Meaning:
Basic research aims at developing theories and general knowledge without immediate practical application.

Characteristics:

  • Knowledge-oriented

  • Long-term value

  • Conducted in academic environments

Example:
Studying theories of consumer motivation.

3.2.2 Applied Research

Meaning:
Applied research focuses on solving immediate, real-life problems.

Characteristics:

  • Practical in nature

  • Problem-oriented

  • Results are directly applicable

Example:
Finding reasons for declining market share of a product.

3.3 Descriptive and Analytical Research

3.3.1 Descriptive Research

  • Describes the current state of affairs

  • No control over variables

  • Often uses surveys and observation

Example:
Studying customer preferences for different brands.

3.3.2 Analytical Research

  • Uses existing data

  • Involves critical analysis and evaluation

Example:
Analyzing financial statements to assess profitability trends.

3.4 Quantitative and Qualitative Research

3.4.1 Quantitative Research

  • Data is expressed in numerical form

  • Uses statistical tools

  • Results are measurable and objective

Example:
Measuring customer satisfaction using rating scales.

3.4.2 Qualitative Research

  • Deals with attitudes, opinions, and motivations

  • Data is descriptive and non-numerical

Example:
In-depth interviews to understand brand perception.

4. Research Process

4.1 Introduction

Research is not a random or unplanned activity. It follows a logical sequence of interrelated steps, collectively known as the research process. Each step builds upon the previous one, and mistakes at an early stage can affect the entire study.

In business research, the research process ensures that problems are clearly defined, data is relevant, and conclusions are valid for decision-making.

4.2 Meaning of Research Process

The research process refers to a series of actions or steps necessary to carry out research systematically and scientifically.

Although the steps appear sequential, in practice they:

  • Overlap

  • Interact with each other

  • Require continuous feedback and revision

4.3 Steps in the Research Process

Step 1: Formulation of the Research Problem

Meaning

This is the first and most critical step. A research problem defines what is to be studied.

A poorly defined problem leads to:

  • Irrelevant data collection

  • Wrong conclusions

  • Waste of time and resources

Sources of Research Problems

  • Business experience

  • Existing literature

  • Discussions with experts

  • Market trends and changes

Example

‚ÄúWhy are sales declining in the northern region despite increased advertising?‚Äù

Step 2: Review of Literature

Meaning

A literature review involves studying:

  • Books

  • Research journals

  • Reports

  • Previous studies related to the topic

Purpose

  • Understand what has already been studied

  • Identify research gaps

  • Avoid duplication

  • Develop theoretical background

Step 3: Formulation of Hypothesis

Meaning

A hypothesis is a tentative assumption or proposition that can be tested.

Example

‚ÄúHigher advertising expenditure leads to increased sales.‚Äù

Characteristics of a Good Hypothesis

  • Clear and precise

  • Testable

  • Consistent with existing theory

Step 4: Research Design

Meaning

A research design is the blueprint of the study. It specifies:

  • Methods of data collection

  • Sampling plan

  • Tools for analysis

Importance

  • Minimizes errors

  • Saves time and cost

  • Improves reliability of results

Step 5: Sample Design

Meaning

Sampling involves selecting a representative subset of the population for study.

Key Decisions

  • Sampling method

  • Sample size

  • Sampling unit

Step 6: Data Collection

Types

  • Primary Data ‚Äì collected first-hand

  • Secondary Data ‚Äì collected from existing sources

Importance

Accuracy of results largely depends on quality of data collected.

Step 7: Execution of the Research

This involves:

  • Actual fieldwork

  • Supervision of data collection

  • Ensuring consistency and accuracy

Step 8: Data Analysis

Activities

  • Editing

  • Coding

  • Classification

  • Tabulation

  • Statistical analysis

Step 9: Hypothesis Testing

Statistical tools are used to:

  • Accept or reject hypotheses

  • Draw inferences about the population

Step 10: Interpretation and Generalization

Interpretation means:

  • Explaining results in simple terms

  • Drawing conclusions

  • Relating findings to objectives

Step 11: Report Writing

The final step involves:

  • Presenting findings

  • Offering recommendations

  • Communicating results to decision-makers

7. Criteria of Good Research

7.1 Introduction

Not all studies qualify as good research. A research study is considered good only when it follows certain scientific and ethical standards. These standards ensure that the findings are reliable, valid, and useful‚Äîespecially important in business where decisions impact profits, people, and policies.

7.2 Essential Criteria of Good Research

7.2.1 Clear and Defined Purpose

  • The research objective must be clearly stated

  • Vague objectives lead to confusion and irrelevant findings

Example:
‚ùå ‚ÄúStudy consumer behavior‚Äù
‚úÖ ‚ÄúStudy factors influencing online purchase decisions among urban youth‚Äù

7.2.2 Proper Research Design

  • A good research design acts as a blueprint

  • It should be appropriate to the nature of the problem

Key requirement:
The design must minimize bias and maximize reliability

7.2.3 Systematic Method

  • Research must follow logical steps

  • Each step should be planned and recorded

Flow:
Problem ‚Üí Data ‚Üí Analysis ‚Üí Conclusion

7.2.4 Objective and Unbiased Approach

  • Research must be free from personal beliefs

  • Findings should depend on facts and evidence, not opinions

7.2.5 Empirical Nature

  • Conclusions must be based on observable and measurable data

  • Guesswork and assumptions should be avoided

7.2.6 Adequate Sample Size

  • Sample must be representative of the population

  • Too small or biased samples lead to misleading results

7.2.7 Valid and Reliable Data

  • Validity ‚Üí Measures what it is supposed to measure

  • Reliability ‚Üí Produces consistent results

AspectMeaning
ValidityAccuracy
ReliabilityConsistency

7.2.8 Logical Analysis

  • Use appropriate statistical and analytical tools

  • Interpret results rationally

7.2.9 Ethical Standards

  • Honest data collection

  • No manipulation of results

  • Confidentiality of respondents

7.2.10 Replicability

  • Another researcher should be able to repeat the study

  • Results should remain largely consistent

Summary of Section 7

Good research is:

  • Systematic

  • Objective

  • Empirical

  • Ethical

  • Replicable

These criteria ensure trustworthy conclusions, especially in business research.

8. Problems Encountered by Researchers

8.1 Introduction

Despite the importance of research, researchers‚Äîparticularly in developing countries like India‚Äîface numerous challenges. These problems affect the quality, timeliness, and usefulness of research outcomes.

8.2 Major Problems Faced by Researchers

8.2.1 Lack of Scientific Training

  • Many researchers lack formal training in research methodology

  • Leads to:

    • Poor problem formulation

    • Faulty analysis

    • Weak conclusions

8.2.2 Inadequate Interaction with Industry

  • Limited collaboration between:

    • Universities

    • Business organizations

    • Government agencies

Result:
Valuable real-world data remains unused.

8.2.3 Non-Availability of Reliable Data

  • Organizations hesitate to share information due to:

    • Fear of misuse

    • Confidentiality concerns

8.2.4 Duplication of Research

  • Lack of centralized research databases

  • Results in:

    • Repetition of studies

    • Waste of resources

8.2.5 Absence of Code of Conduct

  • No uniform ethical guidelines

  • Leads to:

    • Data manipulation

    • Academic rivalry

8.2.6 Inadequate Infrastructure

  • Poor library facilities

  • Lack of statistical software

  • Insufficient technical support

8.2.7 Time and Cost Constraints

  • Research is time-consuming

  • Financial limitations restrict sample size and depth of study

8.2.8 Difficulty in Data Collection

  • Respondents may:

    • Refuse to cooperate

    • Provide inaccurate responses

8.3 Problems Specific to Business Research

  • Rapidly changing markets

  • Consumer behavior volatility

  • Technological disruptions

Summary of Section 8

Researchers face:

  • Methodological

  • Institutional

  • Financial

  • Ethical challenges

Overcoming these requires training, collaboration, and ethical discipline.
