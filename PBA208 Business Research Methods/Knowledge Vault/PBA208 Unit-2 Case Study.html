Designing an Experimental Study to Improve Conversion Rates at Amazon

1. Title

CASE STUDY 2

Evaluating Store Layout Effectiveness Using Quasi-Experimental Design at Walmart

1. Title

Assessing Layout Redesign Impact Using Quasi-Experimental Research

2. Company Background (85 words)

Walmart operates over 10,000 retail stores globally. Store layout significantly influences customer flow, impulse purchases, and sales per square foot. To enhance in-store experience, Walmart redesigned the layout in selected urban stores, repositioning high-margin products near checkout counters.

3. Business Context (95 words)

Due to operational constraints, Walmart could not randomly assign stores to redesigned and non-redesigned categories. Instead, management selected 25 urban stores for redesign based on feasibility. Another 25 comparable stores retained the existing layout. The company must evaluate whether the redesign increased sales of impulse products.

4. Core Business Problem (105 words)

The absence of random assignment introduces potential bias in estimating the true effect of layout redesign. Differences in store location, demographics, and footfall patterns may distort results. The company must design a quasi-experimental approach, possibly using matched-store comparison or difference-in-differences analysis, to estimate impact accurately.

5. Key Data and Metrics (130 words)

Pre-Redesign Average Weekly Impulse Sales:

  • Treatment Stores: $120,000

  • Control Stores: $118,000

Post-Redesign:

  • Treatment Stores: $140,000

  • Control Stores: $122,000

Difference-in-Differences Estimate:

  • Incremental increase: $16,000

Footfall growth:

  • Treatment: 5%

  • Control: 3%

Seasonal variation controlled via historical trends.

6. Decision Situation (65 words)

Walmart must decide whether to scale layout redesign nationally, costing $250 million. The reliability of the quasi-experimental design influences capital investment approval.

7. Analytical Questions

  1. Why is this not a true experimental design?

  2. How does difference-in-differences improve validity?

  3. What biases may still remain?

  4. How can matching techniques enhance reliability?

  5. Should external factors like regional demand be controlled?

8. Model Answers

Q1: Lack of random assignment makes it quasi-experimental.

Q2: Difference-in-differences isolates treatment effect by comparing changes over time across groups.

Q3: Unobserved differences between store types may still bias results.

9. Learning Focus

Highlights quasi-experimental design and policy evaluation logic.

Primary Concepts: Quasi-Experimental Design, Difference-in-Differences, Control Group, Selection Bias, Internal Validity
