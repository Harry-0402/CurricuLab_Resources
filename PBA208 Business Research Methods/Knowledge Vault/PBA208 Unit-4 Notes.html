1. Types of Data

1.1 Introduction

Data is the raw material of research. The quality of research findings depends heavily on the type and quality of data collected. In business research, data is used to analyze markets, understand consumer behavior, evaluate performance, and support managerial decisions.

Broadly, data is classified into:

  1. Primary Data

  2. Secondary Data

2. Primary Data

2.1 Meaning of Primary Data

Primary data is the data collected first-hand by the researcher for a specific research purpose.

Definition

Primary data is original data collected directly from the source by the researcher for the first time.

2.2 Characteristics of Primary Data

  • Original in nature

  • Collected for a specific objective

  • More accurate and reliable

  • Costly and time-consuming

2.3 Advantages of Primary Data

  • High relevance to research objectives

  • Greater accuracy and reliability

  • Better control over data collection process

2.4 Limitations of Primary Data

  • Expensive

  • Time-consuming

  • Requires trained investigators

Example (Business Context)

A company conducts a customer satisfaction survey using questionnaires‚Äîthis data is primary data.

3. Secondary Data

3.1 Meaning of Secondary Data

Secondary data refers to data that has already been:

  • Collected

  • Processed

  • Published

by someone else for a different purpose.

3.2 Sources of Secondary Data

3.2.1 Internal Sources

  • Company records

  • Sales reports

  • Financial statements

3.2.2 External Sources

  • Government publications

  • Research journals

  • Websites and databases

  • Books and reports

3.3 Advantages of Secondary Data

  • Economical

  • Easily available

  • Time-saving

3.4 Limitations of Secondary Data

  • May be outdated

  • Not perfectly suitable for current research

  • Possibility of bias

Summary of Types of Data

  • Primary data is original and specific

  • Secondary data is economical and quick

  • Choice depends on research objectives and resources

4. Methods of Primary Data Collection

4.1 Introduction

Primary data can be collected using various methods depending on:

  • Nature of research

  • Type of respondents

  • Accuracy required

4.2 Observation Method

4.2.1 Meaning

In the observation method, data is collected by:

  • Observing behavior, events, or activities

  • Without asking direct questions

4.2.2 Types of Observation

  • Structured observation

  • Unstructured observation

  • Participant observation

  • Non-participant observation

4.2.3 Advantages of Observation Method

  • Actual behavior is recorded

  • Avoids respondent bias

4.2.4 Limitations of Observation Method

  • Time-consuming

  • Limited scope

  • Observer bias possible

Example

Observing customer movement in a retail store to study buying behavior.

5. Interview Method

5.1 Meaning

An interview involves:

  • Direct interaction between interviewer and respondent

  • Asking questions and recording responses

5.2 Types of Interviews

  • Structured interviews

  • Unstructured interviews

  • Personal interviews

  • Telephonic interviews

5.3 Advantages of Interview Method

  • High response rate

  • Clarification of doubts possible

5.4 Limitations of Interview Method

  • Costly

  • Interviewer bias

  • Time-intensive

Example

Personal interviews with managers to study leadership styles.

6. Questionnaire Method

6.1 Introduction

The questionnaire method is one of the most widely used methods of primary data collection in business research. It involves collecting data by asking respondents a set of written questions designed to obtain specific information.

This method is especially popular in:

  • Marketing research

  • Consumer behavior studies

  • Employee opinion surveys

6.2 Meaning of Questionnaire

A questionnaire is a structured set of questions prepared in advance, which respondents answer in writing.

6.3 Types of Questionnaires

6.3.1 Structured Questionnaire

  • Fixed questions

  • Pre-determined response options

  • Easy to analyze

6.3.2 Unstructured Questionnaire

  • Open-ended questions

  • Allows detailed responses

  • Difficult to analyze

6.4 Types of Questions Used

6.4.1 Open-Ended Questions

  • Respondents answer in their own words

  • Provide qualitative insights

Example:
‚ÄúWhat do you like most about our product?‚Äù

6.4.2 Closed-Ended Questions

  • Limited response options

  • Easy to code and analyze

Example:
‚ÄúHow satisfied are you with our service?‚Äù
(Very satisfied / Satisfied / Neutral / Dissatisfied)

6.5 Advantages of Questionnaire Method

  • Economical and time-saving

  • Suitable for large samples

  • Free from interviewer bias

6.6 Limitations of Questionnaire Method

  • Low response rate

  • No clarification of doubts

  • Possibility of incomplete responses

6.7 Essentials of a Good Questionnaire

  • Simple and clear language

  • Logical sequence of questions

  • Avoid leading and ambiguous questions

  • Proper length

  • Pre-testing (pilot study)

Summary of Questionnaire Method

  • Most common tool in business research

  • Suitable for literate respondents

  • Effectiveness depends on questionnaire design

7. Schedule Method

7.1 Introduction

The schedule method is similar to the questionnaire method, but the key difference lies in who fills the questions.

7.2 Meaning of Schedule

A schedule is a list of questions that is:

  • Filled by the investigator

  • Based on responses given by the respondent during an interview

7.3 Characteristics of Schedule Method

  • Questions are explained by the investigator

  • Suitable for illiterate respondents

  • High response rate

7.4 Advantages of Schedule Method

  • Accurate and complete information

  • Better control over data collection

  • Suitable for rural and semi-literate populations

7.5 Limitations of Schedule Method

  • Costly

  • Time-consuming

  • Requires trained investigators

  • More reliable than questionnaires

  • Widely used in government and social surveys

8. Secondary Data Sources

8.1 Introduction

Secondary data plays an important role when:

  • Primary data collection is expensive

  • Time is limited

  • Historical analysis is required

8.2 Classification of Secondary Data Sources

8.2.1 Internal Sources

  • Sales records

  • Accounting reports

  • Customer databases

  • Inventory records

8.2.2 External Sources

A. Published Sources

  • Government publications (Census, Economic Survey)

  • Research journals

  • Books and magazines

  • Reports of international organizations

B. Unpublished Sources

  • Research theses

  • Company records

  • Private documents

8.3 Precautions While Using Secondary Data

  • Check reliability of source

  • Verify accuracy

  • Ensure relevance to current research

  • Consider time period of data

Summary of Secondary Data Sources

  • Secondary data is quick and economical

  • Must be used carefully to avoid misleading conclusions

9. Measurement

9.1 Introduction

After collecting data, the researcher must ensure that the information is measured accurately and consistently. Measurement is a critical component of research because it determines how abstract concepts are converted into observable data.

In business research, variables such as:

  • Customer satisfaction

  • Brand loyalty

  • Employee motivation

cannot be measured directly. Hence, measurement techniques are used.

9.2 Meaning of Measurement

Measurement refers to the process of assigning numbers or symbols to characteristics of objects according to certain rules.

Definition

Measurement is the systematic assignment of numbers to objects or events according to a set of rules.

9.3 Objectives of Measurement

  • To quantify qualitative attributes

  • To ensure consistency in data collection

  • To facilitate statistical analysis

  • To improve accuracy and objectivity

9.4 Problems in Measurement

  • Abstract nature of variables

  • Respondent bias

  • Instrument errors

  • Ambiguity in questions

Summary of Measurement

  • Measurement bridges the gap between concepts and data

  • Proper measurement ensures valid research conclusions

10. Scaling

10.1 Introduction

A scale is a tool used to measure qualitative variables by converting them into quantitative form.

In business research, scaling is widely used to measure:

  • Attitudes

  • Opinions

  • Perceptions

10.2 Meaning of Scaling

Scaling is the process of placing respondents or objects on a continuum with respect to their attitudes or characteristics.

Example

Measuring customer satisfaction on a scale from:

  • Very dissatisfied ‚Üí Very satisfied

10.3 Types of Measurement Scales

There are four basic scales of measurement:

  1. Nominal Scale

  2. Ordinal Scale

  3. Interval Scale

  4. Ratio Scale

10.4 Nominal Scale

Meaning

  • Classification scale

  • Numbers are used only for identification

Characteristics

  • No order or ranking

  • Only equality or inequality can be shown

Example

  • Gender: Male = 1, Female = 2

  • Type of customer: Retail = 1, Wholesale = 2

Summary

  • Simplest scale

  • Limited statistical operations

10.5 Ordinal Scale

Meaning

  • Data is arranged in rank order

  • Indicates relative position

Characteristics

  • Ranking possible

  • Differences between ranks are not measurable

Example

  • Rank employees as 1st, 2nd, 3rd

  • Customer preference ranking

Summary

  • Shows order but not magnitude

10.6 Interval Scale

Meaning

  • Equal intervals between values

  • No true zero point

Characteristics

  • Addition and subtraction possible

  • Ratios are meaningless

Example

  • Temperature in Celsius

  • Likert scale (Strongly agree ‚Üí Strongly disagree)

Summary

  • Widely used in social and business research

10.7 Ratio Scale

Meaning

  • Possesses all properties of interval scale

  • Has a true zero point

Characteristics

  • All mathematical operations possible

  • Ratios are meaningful

Example

  • Income

  • Sales revenue

  • Age

12. Reliability and Validity

12.1 Introduction

In research, collecting data is not enough‚Äîthe data must be accurate, consistent, and dependable. Two important concepts used to judge the quality of measurement are:

  • Reliability

  • Validity

These concepts ensure that research findings are credible and trustworthy, especially in business decision-making.

13. Reliability

13.1 Meaning of Reliability

Reliability refers to the consistency of a measurement instrument. A measurement is said to be reliable if it produces the same results repeatedly under similar conditions.

Definition

Reliability is the degree to which a measuring instrument yields consistent results.

13.2 Types of Reliability

13.2.1 Test‚ÄìRetest Reliability

  • Same test is administered to the same group at two different times

  • Results are compared

Example:
Administering a job satisfaction survey twice to the same employees.

13.2.2 Split-Half Reliability

  • Test is divided into two equal halves

  • Results of both halves are compared

13.2.3 Inter-Rater Reliability

  • Measures consistency between different observers or interviewers

13.3 Factors Affecting Reliability

  • Ambiguous questions

  • Respondent mood

  • Poorly designed instruments

  • External disturbances

Summary of Reliability

  • Reliability focuses on consistency

  • A tool can be reliable but not necessarily valid

14. Validity

14.1 Meaning of Validity

Validity refers to the accuracy of a measurement instrument‚Äîwhether it measures what it is intended to measure.

Definition

Validity is the extent to which a measuring instrument measures what it claims to measure.

14.2 Types of Validity

14.2.1 Content Validity

  • Measures whether the instrument covers the entire content area

Example:
An exam paper covering all units of a subject.

14.2.2 Construct Validity

  • Measures abstract concepts like:

    • Intelligence

    • Motivation

    • Attitude

14.2.3 Criterion-Related Validity

  • Measurement compared with an external criterion

14.3 Relationship Between Reliability and Validity

Summary of Validity

  • Validity ensures meaningful interpretation of results

  • Crucial for accurate business decisions
