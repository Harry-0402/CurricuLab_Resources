Part A: Sale of Goods Act, 1930

1. Introduction & Contract of Sale

Concept: The Act governs contracts where ownership of movable goods is transferred for a price. It does not apply to immovable property (land/buildings).

Definition (Section 4(1)):

"A contract of sale of goods is a contract whereby the seller transfers or agrees to transfer the property in goods to the buyer for a price."

Essential Elements:

  1. Two Parties: Buyer and Seller.
  2. Goods: Must be movable property.
  3. Price: Money consideration (Barter is not a sale).
  4. Transfer of Ownership: Ownership must shift.

5. Implied Conditions (Automatic Protections)

Even if not written, the law assumes these conditions exist to protect the buyer:

  1. Condition as to Title (Section 14(a)): Seller actually owns the goods. (If stolen, buyer gets money back). Example: A sells a stolen laptop to B. Contract is void.
  2. As to Description (Section 15): Goods must match the description (e.g., "Egyptian Cotton"). Example: Selling “Egyptian cotton” but delivering ordinary cotton.
  3. As to Sample (Section 17): Bulk must match the sample quality.
  4. As to Fitness (Section 16(1)): If buyer relies on seller's skill for a specific purpose, goods must fit that purpose.
  5. Merchantable Quality (Section 16(2)): Goods must be fit for resale/ordinary use (e.g., a watch must tell time).

 

6.2 Implied Warranties

Meaning: Implied warranties are collateral terms which do not affect the core of the contract.

Types of Implied Warranties

  1.      Warranty of Quiet Possession: Buyer should enjoy goods without disturbance.
  2.      Warranty of Freedom from Encumbrances: Goods should be free from any charge or lien.
  3.      Warranty as to Disclosure of Dangerous Nature: Seller must warn buyer about dangerous nature of goods.

9. Performance of a Contract of Sale

Introduction

After a contract of sale is formed, both buyer and seller are expected to perform their respective duties. Performance mainly relates to delivery of goods by the seller and payment of price by the buyer.

Meaning of Performance: Performance of a contract of sale means:

Delivery of Goods (Section 33): Delivery means voluntary transfer of possession from seller to buyer.

Modes of Delivery

  1. Actual Delivery – Physical handing over of goods
  2. Symbolic Delivery – Delivery through symbol (e.g., keys of warehouse)
  3. Constructive Delivery – Third party acknowledges holding goods for buyer

Acceptance of Goods (Section 42)

Buyer is deemed to accept goods when:

  1. He intimates acceptance
  2. He does any act inconsistent with seller’s ownership
  3. Retains goods beyond reasonable time

10. Rights of Unpaid Seller

Introduction: A seller is called an unpaid seller when:

‚óè        Whole price is not paid, or Conditional payment instrument is dishonoured

The Act gives special rights to protect the seller.

Unpaid Seller (Section 45): A seller is "unpaid" if the full price is not paid or a cheque bounces.

Rights of Unpaid Seller:

  1. Against Goods (Right in Rem):
    • Lien: Keep possession of goods until paid.
    • Stoppage in Transit: Stop the truck/ship if buyer becomes insolvent while goods are moving.
    • Resale: Sell to someone else (if perishable or after notice).
  2. Against Buyer (Right in Personam):
    • Suit for Price.
    • Suit for Damages.
    • Suit for Interest.

11. Remedies for Breach of Contract of Sale

Remedies Available to Seller

  1. Suit for price
  2. Suit for damages
  3. Suit for interest
  4. Resale of goods

Remedies Available to Buyer

  1. Suit for damages
  2. Suit for specific performance
  3. Suit for breach of warranty
  4. Repudiation of contract

UNIT II (Part B): Law of Partnership (Indian Partnership Act, 1932)

12. Introduction to Partnership

When two or more persons join together to carry on a business and share profits, the relationship is called partnership. The Indian Partnership Act, 1932 governs partnership firms in India.

 Definition and Nature of Partnership

Definition (Section 4): “Partnership is the relation between persons who have agreed to share profits of a business carried on by all or any of them acting for all.”

Essential Elements

  1. Agreement: Must be voluntary (Oral or Written).
  2. Number of Persons: Minimum 2.
  3. Business: Must be a lawful trade/occupation.
  4. Profit Sharing: Must agree to share profits (Loss sharing is not mandatory).
  5. Mutual Agency: (Crucial Element).

üìå Mutual agency is the true test of partnership.

14. Formation of Partnership

Introduction: A partnership is formed by agreement, not by law.

15. Rights and Liabilities of Partners

Rights of Partners: To take part in business, access books, share profits, and be indemnified (reimbursed) for expenses.

Liabilities of Partners: Liability is Unlimited and Joint & Several. (Creditors can recover debt from any one partner's personal assets).

16. Dissolution of Partnership Firm

Introduction: Dissolution means termination of partnership relationship (The firm closes down completely.).

Modes of Dissolution

  1. Mutual Agreement: All partners agree to close.
  2. Compulsory: Insolvency of all partners or unlawful business.
  3.  By Notice: In "Partnership at Will", any partner can give notice to quit.
  4. By Court Order: If a partner becomes of unsound mind or guilty of misconduct.
  5. By death
  6. By insolvency

Effects of Dissolution
