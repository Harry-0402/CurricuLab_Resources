UNIT I: Law of Contract (Indian Contract Act, 1872)

 

1. Meaning and Nature of Contract

Introduction

Business revolves around promises. When these promises are legally recognized and enforced, they become contracts. The Indian Contract Act, 1872 governs these rules in India.

What is a Contract? 

Contract (Section 2(h)): "A contract is an agreement enforceable by law." 

When an agreement is legally enforceable, it becomes a contract.

To understand this, use this simple formula: Contract = Agreement + Enforceability by Law 

 Agreement (Section 2(e)): Every promise and every set of promises, forming the consideration for each other. (Offer + Acceptance).

‚óè       There must be an agreement between two or more parties.

 Enforceability: The agreement must be legally binding.

To understand it simply:

  1. Offer + Acceptance = Promise
  2. Promise + Consideration = Agreement
  3. Agreement + Legal Enforceability = Contract

Note: All contracts are agreements, but all agreements are not contracts. 

Social or moral agreements (like inviting a friend to dinner) lack legal obligations and are not contracts.

3. Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

Introduction

Not every agreement becomes a contract. The Indian Contract Act specifies certain essential elements that must be present for an agreement to be legally valid.

According to Section 10, for an agreement to be a contract, it must have the following:

  1. Offer and Acceptance: There must be a lawful offer and a lawful acceptance.
  2. Lawful Consideration: Something in return that is legal.
  3. Capacity to Contract: Parties must be adults and of sound mind.
  4. Free Consent: Agreement must be without pressure or fraud.
  5. Lawful Object: The purpose must not be illegal or immoral.
  6. Not Expressly Declared Void: It shouldn't be a type of agreement the law specifically bans.
  7. Certainty & Possibility: The terms must be clear and the act possible to do.
  8. Legal formalities (where required)

üìå Absence of any one element makes the contract invalid.

4. Offer and Acceptance

Offer (Proposal)

‚óè        Definition (Section 2(a)): When a person signifies his willingness to do or abstain from doing something to obtain assent (approval) from another.

‚óè        Essentials: It must be clear, communicated, and made with the intention to create a legal relationship.

‚óè        Example: A offers to sell his bike to B for ‚Çπ50,000.

4.2 Acceptance

‚óè        Definition (Section 2(b)): When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent.

‚óè        Essentials: Must be absolute, unconditional, communicated, and within a reasonable time.

‚óè        Mirror Image Rule: Acceptance must exactly match the offer terms.

5. Consideration

Introduction

In contract law, a promise without something in return is generally not enforceable. This “something in return” is called consideration. Consideration gives value to a promise and makes it legally binding.

‚óè        Meaning (Section 2(d)): Consideration is the "price paid for a promise." It is doing (or abstaining from) something at the desire of the promisor.

‚óè        Rule: "No Consideration, No Contract" (Exception: Natural love and affection, gifts, etc.).

üìå Simple meaning: Consideration is the price paid for a promise.

Exceptions: Agreements Without Consideration

As per Section 25, agreements without consideration are void, except:

  2. Compensation for past voluntary service
  3. Promise to pay time-barred debt
  4. Completed gift
  5. Creation of agency

6. Capacity to Contract

A person is competent to contract if they fulfil Section 11:

  1. Age of Majority:Must be 18+ years.
    • Note: A contract with a minor is Void Ab Initio (void from the start).
  2. Sound Mind: Can understand the contract and form rational judgment (not drunk or mentally ill).
  3. Not Disqualified: Not an alien enemy, insolvent, or convict.

7. Free Consent

Introduction

For a contract to be valid, consent must be given freely. Consent obtained by unfair means is not genuine and affects the validity of the contract.

Meaning of Consent

As per Section 13:

“Two or more persons are said to consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense.”

Consent is "free" when it is NOT caused by:

  1. Coercion (Section 15): Committing or threatening a crime (e.g., kidnapping) to get consent. Effect: Voidable.
  2. Undue Influence (Section 16): Using a dominant position (e.g., Doctor-Patient, Parent-Child) to gain unfair advantage. Effect: Voidable.
  3. Fraud (Section 17): Intentionally deceiving someone or hiding facts. Effect: Voidable + Damages.
  4. Misrepresentation (Section 18): Innocent false statement without intent to deceive. Effect: Voidable (no damages).
  5. Mistake:
    • Mistake of Fact (Bilateral): Both parties are mistaken about a core fact. Effect: Void.

8. Legality of Object and Consideration

Introduction

Even if all other elements of a contract are present, the contract will be void if its object or consideration is unlawful. Law does not enforce agreements that go against public interest or morality.

Meaning

According to Section 23 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872,
The consideration or object of an agreement is unlawful if it:

  1. Is forbidden by law
  2. Defeats the provisions of any law
  3. Is fraudulent
  4. Involves injury to person or property
  5. Is immoral
  6. Is opposed to public policy

üìå If the object or consideration is unlawful, the agreement is void.

9. Contingent Contracts

‚óè        Definition (Section 31): A contract to do or not do something if an uncertain future event happens.

‚óè        Example: A promises to pay B ‚Çπ10,000 if B's house is burnt. This is insurance.

‚óè        Rule: It is enforceable only when the event happens. If the event becomes impossible, the contract is void.

10. Discharge of Contract

Introduction

Discharge of contract means termination of contractual obligations. Once discharged, parties are no longer bound.

A contract ends (is discharged) by:

  1. Performance: Everyone did their job.
  2. Mutual Agreement: Both agree to cancel.
  3. Lapse of Time: Deadline passed.
  4. Impossibility (Section 56): The subject matter is destroyed or law changes.
  5. Breach: One party failed to perform.

11. Quasi Contracts

üìå Quasi contracts are not real contracts, but they are treated as contracts by law.

‚óè        Concept: Not a real contract. It is an obligation imposed by law to prevent "Unjust Enrichment" (getting rich at another's expense unfairly).

‚óè        Example (Finder of Goods): If A finds B's wallet, A must return it (same responsibility as a bailee)
