The company‚Äôs business model was deeply contract-driven. NexaCart typically entered into multi-year logistics service agreements specifying delivery timelines, service-level agreements (SLAs), penalties for delays, indemnity provisions, and termination rights. 2. Business Environment and Context

India‚Äôs e-commerce and organized retail sectors have witnessed intense competition over the last decade. Consumer expectations around delivery speed, reliability, and cost have increased significantly. To meet these expectations, retailers have outsourced logistics operations to specialized service providers with scale and geographic reach.

Third-party logistics providers operate on thin margins and depend on volume commitments from retailers. Contracts often include minimum volume guarantees, performance-linked pricing, and penalty clauses for service failures. However, fluctuating demand, fuel price volatility, and labor shortages introduce uncertainty into performance outcomes.

Customer behavior in the retail sector is highly sensitive to delivery experience. Delays or service disruptions directly impact customer retention and brand reputation. As a result, retailers like NexaCart impose stringent SLAs on logistics partners, sometimes without fully accounting for external constraints.

From a legal perspective, logistics outsourcing contracts are governed by general principles of contract law. Issues commonly arise regarding the certainty of offer and acceptance, consideration, intention to create legal relations, and interpretation of performance obligations. Informal communications such as emails, purchase orders, and operational instructions often complicate determination of contractual intent.

Technological integration has further blurred contractual boundaries. Real-time dashboards, automated dispatch systems, and digital confirmations create operational dependencies that may or may not be contractually documented. Disputes frequently arise when one party treats operational coordination as evidence of a binding contract, while the other views it as preliminary or conditional.

In this environment, NexaCart sought to rapidly onboard logistics partners to support expansion into Tier-2 cities. One such onboarding decision, made under commercial pressure, resulted in a dispute that tested the very foundations of contract formation under Indian law.

3. Emerging Business Problem

In January 2023, NexaCart initiated discussions with SwiftHaul Logistics LLP (SHL), a regional 3PL provider operating across southern India. NexaCart intended to outsource last-mile delivery operations for eight Tier-2 cities as part of a rapid expansion plan ahead of the festive season.

Initial discussions were conducted through meetings and email exchanges. NexaCart shared draft service requirements, expected delivery volumes, and indicative pricing. SHL responded with a commercial proposal outlining rates, service commitments, and operational assumptions. The proposal included a clause stating that the terms were ‚Äúsubject to execution of a formal agreement.‚Äù

Due to internal urgency, NexaCart‚Äôs operations team instructed SHL to commence pilot operations in March 2023, even though the final contract had not been signed. Email confirmations were exchanged approving rates and service areas. Over the next four months, SHL handled significant delivery volumes and raised monthly invoices.

By July 2023, operational issues emerged. Delivery delays increased due to labor shortages and vehicle unavailability. NexaCart imposed deductions from invoices citing SLA breaches. SHL contested these deductions, arguing that SLAs and penalty clauses were never mutually finalized.

The dispute escalated when NexaCart terminated the arrangement citing breach of contract and withheld outstanding payments of approximately INR 18 crore. SHL issued a legal notice claiming that a valid contract existed based on offer, acceptance, and performance, and demanded payment along with damages for wrongful termination.

Internally, NexaCart‚Äôs management was divided. The operations team believed that emails and conduct constituted a binding contract. The legal team argued that absence of a formally executed agreement weakened enforceability of penalty and termination clauses.

The company now faced legal uncertainty regarding whether a valid contract existed, what its terms were, and what remedies were available to either party under the Indian Contract Act.

5. Internal Challenges

NexaCart‚Äôs internal challenges stemmed from process gaps and cultural priorities. Speed of execution was valued over procedural discipline. Operations teams frequently initiated services based on commercial emails, assuming legal formalities could follow later.

There was no centralized contract repository or tracking mechanism. Different versions of draft agreements circulated without clarity on which terms were agreed. The legal team was often consulted after disputes arose rather than during contract formation.

Additionally, there was limited awareness among managers about essential elements of a valid contract, particularly acceptance, intention, and certainty of terms. This gap increased the risk of disputes and weakened NexaCart‚Äôs bargaining position when conflicts emerged.
