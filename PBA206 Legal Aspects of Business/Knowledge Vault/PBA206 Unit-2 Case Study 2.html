2. Business Environment and Context

India‚Äôs marine exports sector is highly regulated and quality-sensitive. Overseas buyers impose stringent standards related to freshness, contamination levels, packaging, and temperature control. Any deviation can result in rejection, price reductions, or blacklisting of suppliers.

Contracts for the sale of goods in this sector are complex due to:

  • Long transit times
  • Perishability of goods
  • Dependence on cold-chain logistics
  • Strict inspection rights of buyers

Buyers often reserve the right to inspect goods at the destination port and reject consignments that do not conform to specifications. Sellers, on the other hand, attempt to transfer ownership and risk as early as possible to minimize exposure.

The legal distinction between a sale and an agreement to sell, and the timing of passing of property and risk, becomes critical in export transactions. Disputes frequently arise when goods deteriorate during transit or storage after shipment.

In partnership firms, these risks are amplified due to the principle of unlimited and joint liability. External parties typically rely on representations made by individual partners, assuming authority based on the doctrine of mutual agency.

In recent years, increased scrutiny by overseas regulators and buyers has resulted in higher rejection rates and stricter enforcement of contractual terms. Exporters like BEP face pressure to accept buyer-favorable clauses to retain market access.

Against this backdrop, BEP entered into a major export contract that appeared commercially attractive but carried significant hidden legal risks.

3. Emerging Business Problem

In February 2023, BEP entered into a contract with OceanMart Trading LLC, a Dubai-based seafood distributor, for the supply of frozen shrimp valued at USD 11.8 million (approximately INR 98 crore). The contract covered monthly shipments over a six-month period.

The agreement specified:

  • Detailed quality parameters
  • CIF delivery terms
  • Payment through an irrevocable LC payable 45 days after shipment
  • A clause stating that goods were ‚Äúsubject to inspection and approval at destination‚Äù

Between March and May 2023, BEP shipped consignments worth INR 42 crore. All shipments were accompanied by quality certificates issued by an independent inspection agency at the Indian port.

In June 2023, OceanMart informed BEP that a major consignment had failed quality checks at Dubai due to alleged temperature fluctuations and bacterial contamination. OceanMart rejected the goods, refused payment under the LC, and demanded compensation for losses.

BEP disputed the claim, arguing that:

  • Goods met quality standards at the time of shipment
  • Risk had passed to the buyer once goods were loaded and shipped
  • Any deterioration occurred due to handling at destination

OceanMart invoked the inspection clause and claimed that ownership had not passed due to conditional acceptance. Simultaneously, one of BEP‚Äôs partners assured OceanMart of replacement shipments without consulting other partners, leading to internal conflict.

The dispute resulted in blocked payments, mounting storage costs at the destination port, and threats of legal action. The partners faced disagreement over strategy‚Äîwhether to litigate, settle, or absorb losses to preserve the buyer relationship.
