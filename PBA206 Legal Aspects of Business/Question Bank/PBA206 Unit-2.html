Question:

Explain the rights of an unpaid seller under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

Answer (15 Marks):

The Big Picture (Why this topic is very important)

In business, goods are often sold on credit. This means the seller delivers the goods first and expects payment later.
But what if the buyer:

  • Does not pay the price?

  • Refuses to accept goods?

  • Becomes insolvent?

If the law did not protect sellers, trade would become very risky.
That is why the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 gives special rights to an unpaid seller.

Think of it like this:

If you have done your duty honestly, the law will not leave you helpless.

Who is an Unpaid Seller?

A seller is called an unpaid seller when:

  1. The whole price has not been paid, or

  2. Payment is made by cheque or bill, and it is dishonoured

Even if part payment is received, the seller is still unpaid for the balance amount.

Classification of Rights of an Unpaid Seller

The rights of an unpaid seller are divided into two main categories:

  1. Rights against the goods

  2. Rights against the buyer personally

This classification is very important for exams.

I. Rights of an Unpaid Seller Against the Goods

These rights allow the seller to control, stop, or resell the goods.

1. Right of Lien

The right of lien means the right of the seller to retain possession of goods until the full price is paid.

When can lien be exercised?

  • Sale without credit

  • Credit period has expired

  • Buyer becomes insolvent

When does lien end?

  • When goods are delivered to a carrier

  • When buyer or his agent lawfully obtains possession

  • When seller waives his right

Example:
A furniture seller keeps the sofa in his shop until the buyer pays fully.

2. Right of Stoppage in Transit

If goods have been delivered to a carrier and the buyer becomes insolvent, the seller can stop the goods while they are in transit.

Conditions

  • Buyer must be insolvent

  • Goods must still be in transit

Example:
Goods are on the way by truck, but buyer is declared bankrupt. Seller can stop delivery.

3. Right of Resale

The unpaid seller can resell the goods in the following cases:

  • Goods are perishable

  • Seller gives notice to buyer and buyer does not pay

  • Right of resale is reserved in the contract

Effect of resale

  • Seller can recover loss

  • Buyer loses ownership rights

Example:
Reselling fruits to avoid spoilage.

II. Rights of an Unpaid Seller Against the Buyer Personally

These rights allow the seller to take legal action in court.

4. Suit for Price

The seller can file a suit for price when:

  • Ownership has passed to the buyer, and

  • Buyer fails to pay the price

Example:
Goods delivered, but buyer refuses to pay.

5. Suit for Damages for Non-Acceptance

If the buyer wrongfully refuses to accept goods, the seller can claim damages for loss suffered.

6. Suit for Damages for Repudiation

If the buyer cancels the contract before the due date, the seller can immediately sue for damages.

Example:
Buyer cancels bulk order just before delivery.

7. Suit for Interest

The seller can claim interest on unpaid price, if:

  • Provided in contract, or

  • Allowed by the court

Business Case Study (Exam-Friendly)

A wholesaler supplies goods worth ‚Çπ5 lakh on credit.
The buyer‚Äôs cheque bounces and he becomes insolvent.

The seller can:

  • Hold remaining goods (lien)

  • Stop goods in transit

  • Resell goods

  • Sue for price, damages, and interest

This shows how strongly the law protects unpaid sellers.

Advantages of These Rights

  • Protect sellers from dishonest buyers

  • Encourage timely payment

  • Reduce business risk

  • Promote fair trade

Final Takeaway (Conclusion)

The Sale of Goods Act ensures that an unpaid seller is never helpless.
He can retain goods, stop delivery, resell goods, or approach the court to recover his money.

In one strong line:

The law makes sure that sellers are paid‚Äîor protected‚Äîfor what they sell.

Question:

Explain the rules relating to delivery of goods under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

Answer (15 Marks):

The Big Picture (Why this topic is very important)

In a contract of sale, delivery is the practical completion of the deal.

Delivery means the voluntary transfer of possession of goods from the seller to the buyer.

üëâ It is about possession, not ownership.
Ownership and delivery may happen at the same time or at different times.

Rules Relating to Delivery of Goods

1. Delivery and Payment are Concurrent

Unless otherwise agreed:

  • The seller must be ready to deliver goods, and

  • The buyer must be ready to pay the price

Both happen at the same time.

Example:
A shopkeeper hands over goods only after receiving payment.

2. Mode of Delivery

Delivery can be made in three ways:

(a) Actual Delivery

Goods are physically handed over to the buyer.

Example:
Giving a mobile phone directly to the buyer.

(b) Symbolic Delivery

Something representing the goods is delivered.

Example:
Handing over keys of a godown or warehouse receipt.

(c) Constructive Delivery

Goods are with a third person, who agrees to hold them on behalf of the buyer.

Example:
Goods lying in a warehouse.

3. Place of Delivery

  • If the place is fixed in the contract ‚Üí delivery must be made there

  • If no place is mentioned ‚Üí delivery is made at the seller‚Äôs place of business

Example:
If nothing is agreed, buyer must collect goods from seller‚Äôs shop.

4. Time of Delivery

  • If time is fixed ‚Üí delivery must be made at that time

  • If no time is fixed ‚Üí delivery must be made within a reasonable time

What is reasonable depends on:

  • Nature of goods

  • Circumstances of the case

Example:
Delay in delivering milk is unreasonable.

5. Delivery of Wrong Quantity

(a) Less Quantity

Buyer may:

  • Reject goods, or

  • Accept goods and pay proportionately

(b) Excess Quantity

Buyer may:

  • Reject excess goods, or

  • Accept agreed quantity only, or

  • Accept all and pay for all

(c) Mixed Goods

Buyer may:

  • Reject whole delivery, or

  • Accept goods as per contract and reject the rest

The buyer is not bound to accept delivery in instalments unless agreed in the contract.

Example:
If contract says ‚Äúsingle delivery,‚Äù seller cannot force instalments.

7. Delivery to Carrier or Wharfinger

Delivery of goods to a carrier for transmission to the buyer is treated as delivery to the buyer, unless otherwise agreed.

8. Expenses of Delivery

  • Seller bears expenses of making goods ready

  • Buyer bears expenses of taking delivery

9. Effect of Part Delivery

Part delivery may be treated as delivery of the whole depending on intention of parties.

  • Reduce disputes

  • Ensure smooth trade

The rules relating to delivery of goods clearly explain how, when, and where goods must change hands.

Delivery completes the physical side of a contract of sale.

Question:

Explain the rules relating to transfer of ownership of goods under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

In a contract of sale, the most crucial question is not delivery, but this:
üëâ When does the buyer become the real owner of the goods?

This moment is called the transfer of ownership (or transfer of property in goods).
It is extremely important because risk follows ownership. Whoever owns the goods bears the loss if the goods are damaged or destroyed.

Think of it like buying a bike. Once it becomes legally yours, any accident or damage after that point is your responsibility, not the seller‚Äôs.

Meaning of Transfer of Ownership

Transfer of ownership means the passing of property (legal ownership) in goods from the seller to the buyer.

üìå Important point:
Ownership is different from possession.
A person may possess goods but may not be the owner.

General Rule: Intention of the Parties

The main rule under the Sale of Goods Act is:

Ownership passes when the parties intend it to pass.

This intention is understood from:

  • Terms of the contract

  • Conduct of buyer and seller

  • Circumstances of the case

Rules Relating to Transfer of Ownership

1. Specific Goods in a Deliverable State

If the contract is for specific goods which are already in a deliverable state, ownership passes at the time of making the contract, even if:

  • Payment is postponed, or

  • Delivery is delayed

Example:
Buying a ready-made refrigerator from a showroom‚Äîownership passes immediately.

2. Specific Goods Not in a Deliverable State

If goods require some work (like packing, weighing, or repairing), ownership passes only when:

  • The required work is completed, and

  • The buyer is informed

Example:
Rice must be weighed before delivery‚Äîownership passes after weighing.

3. Specific Goods to Be Put into a Deliverable State

If the seller must do something to make goods deliverable, ownership passes after the act is done and buyer is informed.

Example:
A machine needs assembly before delivery.

4. Unascertained or Future Goods

In the case of unascertained goods, ownership does not pass until:

  • Goods are identified, and

  • Goods are unconditionally set aside for the buyer

Example:
Selecting 50 bags of cement from a large stock.

5. Goods Sent on Approval or Sale or Return

Ownership passes when:

  • Buyer approves the goods, or

  • Buyer keeps goods beyond reasonable time without rejection

Example:
Clothes taken home for trial and not returned.

6. Reservation of Right of Disposal

If the seller reserves ownership until certain conditions are fulfilled (like payment), ownership passes only after those conditions are satisfied.

Example:
Ownership passes only after full payment, even if goods are delivered.

7. Risk Follows Ownership

Once ownership passes to the buyer:

  • Risk also passes to the buyer

  • Loss is buyer‚Äôs responsibility, even if goods are with seller

Exception:
If loss is due to seller‚Äôs fault, seller remains liable.

Business Case Study (Exam-Oriented)

A seller sells machinery and agrees that ownership will pass only after full payment.
Even if the machine is delivered, ownership remains with the seller until payment is completed.

If the machine is damaged before payment:

  • Seller bears the loss

Advantages of These Rules

  • Clearly decide responsibility

  • Reduce disputes

  • Protect both buyer and seller

  • Bring certainty to business contracts

Final Takeaway (Conclusion)

The transfer of ownership depends mainly on:

  • Intention of the parties

  • Nature and condition of goods

  • Completion of agreed conditions

It decides who bears the risk and responsibility in a contract of sale.

In one strong line:

Ownership decides risk, responsibility, and rights.

Question:

Explain the performance of a contract of sale under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

Answer (15 Marks):

The Big Picture (Why this topic is very important)

A contract of sale does not end just by making an agreement.
It is considered properly completed only when both parties actually do what they promised.

In simple words:

  • The seller must deliver the goods, and

  • The buyer must accept the goods and pay the price

This process is called the performance of a contract of sale.

Think of it like a handshake deal. Talking is not enough‚Äîaction matters.

Meaning of Performance of Contract of Sale

Performance of a contract of sale means:

  • The seller delivers the goods as agreed, and

  • The buyer accepts the goods and pays the price

If either party fails, the contract is not properly performed.

Duties of the Seller

1. Duty to Deliver the Goods

The seller must deliver the goods:

  • At the right place

  • At the right time

  • In the right condition

Delivery must follow the terms of the contract.

Example:
If delivery is promised on Monday, delivering on Friday is a breach.

2. Duty to Make Goods Ready for Delivery

The seller must bear expenses related to:

  • Packing

  • Weighing

  • Measuring

These are needed to make goods ready for delivery.

3. Duty to Deliver Goods According to Contract

Goods must:

  • Match the description

  • Match the sample (if applicable)

  • Be of agreed quality

Wrong or defective goods amount to improper performance.

Duties of the Buyer

4. Duty to Accept the Goods

The buyer must be ready and willing to take delivery when the seller performs his duty.

Unreasonable refusal to accept goods is a breach by the buyer.

5. Duty to Pay the Price

The buyer must pay the agreed price:

  • At the time fixed in the contract, or

  • At the time of delivery (if no time is fixed)

6. Duty to Bear Expenses of Taking Delivery

The buyer bears expenses related to:

  • Transport after delivery

  • Unloading

  • Storage after acceptance

Rules Relating to Performance

7. Delivery and Payment are Concurrent Conditions

Unless otherwise agreed:

  • Seller must be ready to deliver, and

  • Buyer must be ready to pay

Both must happen at the same time.

8. Mode of Delivery

Delivery may be:

  • Actual ‚Äì physical handover

  • Symbolic ‚Äì keys or documents

  • Constructive ‚Äì third party holds goods for buyer

9. Acceptance of Goods

Goods are said to be accepted when:

  • Buyer informs acceptance, or

  • Buyer uses the goods, or

  • Buyer keeps goods without rejecting for reasonable time

Once accepted, goods cannot be rejected.

Business Case Study (Exam-Friendly)

A seller delivers a washing machine:

  • Seller installs it properly

  • Buyer inspects, accepts, and pays

This completes performance.

If buyer refuses payment after delivery ‚Üí buyer breaches the contract.
If seller delivers a damaged machine ‚Üí seller breaches the contract.

Importance of Performance

  • Ensures smooth trade

  • Reduces disputes

  • Builds trust between buyer and seller

  • Brings contracts to a legal end

Final Takeaway (Conclusion)

Performance of a contract of sale is complete only when:

  • The seller delivers the goods properly, and

  • The buyer accepts the goods and pays the price

Both sides must cooperate for successful completion of the sale.

In one strong line:

A sale is complete only when promises turn into actions.

Explain the remedial measures available to the buyer under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

The Big Picture (Why this topic is very important)

In business, buyers often pay money before or at the time of delivery. But sometimes sellers:

  • Do not deliver goods

  • Deliver goods late

  • Deliver defective or wrong goods

If buyers had no protection, trade would be unfair and risky.
That is why the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 provides strong remedial measures to buyers.

Think of these remedies as legal tools that help buyers recover loss or protect their rights when sellers fail to perform their duties.

What are Buyer‚Äôs Remedies?

Buyer‚Äôs remedies are the legal rights available to the buyer when the seller commits a breach of contract.

These remedies allow the buyer to:

  • Reject goods

  • Cancel the contract

  • Recover money

  • Claim compensation

Remedial Measures Available to the Buyer

1. Suit for Damages for Non-Delivery

If the seller fails or refuses to deliver goods, the buyer can file a suit for damages.

Meaning

Damages are monetary compensation for the loss suffered due to non-delivery.

Example:
You pay for furniture, but the seller never delivers it. You can claim damages for inconvenience and loss.

2. Suit for Specific Performance

In special cases, the buyer can ask the court to order the seller to deliver the goods, instead of paying damages.

When applicable

  • Goods are unique or rare

  • Money compensation is not sufficient

Example:
Purchase of a rare painting, antique item, or custom-made machinery.

3. Suit for Breach of Warranty

If the seller breaches a warranty (minor term), the buyer:

  • Cannot cancel the contract

  • Can claim damages

Example:
Wrong colour, minor defect, or small variation in goods.

4. Suit for Breach of Condition

If the seller breaches a condition (essential term), the buyer can:

  • Reject the goods

  • Cancel the contract

  • Claim damages

Example:
Second-hand goods delivered instead of new goods.

5. Right to Reject Goods

The buyer has the right to reject goods if:

  • Goods are defective

  • Goods do not match description

  • Goods do not match sample

This right must be exercised within a reasonable time.

6. Suit for Recovery of Price

If the buyer has already paid the price and the seller fails to deliver goods, the buyer can:

  • Recover the amount paid

  • Claim interest if allowed

7. Right to Examine Goods

Before accepting goods, the buyer has the right to examine them to ensure they match the contract.

The buyer is not considered to have accepted goods until he gets this opportunity.

8. Suit for Interest

The buyer can claim interest on the amount paid, if:

  • Provided in the contract, or

  • Allowed by the court

Business Case Study (Exam-Friendly)

A buyer orders 100 computers for office use:

  • Seller delivers only 60

  • Some computers are defective

The buyer can:

  • Reject defective computers

  • Accept remaining goods and pay proportionately

  • Claim damages for loss

  • Recover excess payment

This shows how buyer‚Äôs remedies work in real business.

Advantages of Buyer‚Äôs Remedies

  • Protect buyers from unfair loss

  • Promote honest trade practices

  • Build confidence in market transactions

  • Ensure seller accountability

Final Takeaway (Conclusion)

The Sale of Goods Act ensures that buyers are never helpless.
If the seller fails in his duty, the buyer can reject goods, recover money, or claim compensation, depending on the seriousness of the breach.

In one strong line:

The law protects buyers when sellers fail to keep their promises.

Explain the rules relating to acceptance of goods under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

In a contract of sale, delivery alone does not complete the transaction.

You receive an online order, open it, start using it, and keep it. At that moment, the law assumes you have accepted the goods.

That is why the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 lays down clear rules about acceptance of goods.

Meaning of Acceptance of Goods

A buyer is said to have accepted the goods when:

  • He intimates acceptance to the seller, or

  • He does any act inconsistent with the ownership of the seller, or

  • He retains the goods for a reasonable time without rejecting them

Acceptance shows that the buyer is satisfied with the goods.

Rules Relating to Acceptance of Goods

1. Buyer Has the Right to Examine the Goods

Before acceptance, the buyer has the right to examine the goods to check whether:

  • They match the description

  • They match the sample

  • They are free from defects

The buyer is not deemed to have accepted the goods until he gets a reasonable opportunity to examine them.

Example:
Checking electronic items after delivery.

2. Acceptance by Express Communication

If the buyer clearly informs the seller that he has accepted the goods, acceptance is complete.

Example:
Sending a message or email saying, ‚ÄúGoods received and accepted.‚Äù

3. Acceptance by Conduct

If the buyer performs any act that shows ownership, acceptance is presumed.

Such acts include:

  • Using the goods

  • Reselling the goods

  • Pledging or mortgaging the goods

Example:
Installing and using a washing machine.

4. Acceptance by Retaining Goods

If the buyer keeps the goods beyond a reasonable time without rejecting them, acceptance is assumed.

What is ‚Äúreasonable time‚Äù depends on:

  • Nature of goods

  • Circumstances of the case

Example:
Keeping delivered goods for many days without complaint.

5. Partial Acceptance

The buyer may accept part of the goods and reject the rest.

Acceptance applies only to the accepted goods.

Example:
Accepting 90 good items and rejecting 10 defective items.

6. Effect of Acceptance of Goods

Once goods are accepted:

  • Buyer must pay the price

  • Buyer cannot reject the goods

  • Buyer can only claim damages for defects

7. Buyer‚Äôs Responsibility After Acceptance

After acceptance, the buyer must:

  • Take reasonable care of goods

  • Not damage or misuse them

8. Acceptance in Instalment Delivery

If goods are delivered in instalments, acceptance of one instalment does not automatically mean acceptance of all, unless agreed otherwise.

Explain the rules relating to delivery of wrong quantity of goods under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930.

The Big Picture (Why this topic is very important)

In real business life, delivery mistakes are very common. A seller may deliver:

  • Less quantity than agreed

  • More quantity than agreed

  • Goods mixed with other goods of a different description

If the buyer were forced to accept such goods, trade would become unfair.
That is why the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 clearly lays down rules relating to delivery of wrong quantity.

These rules mainly protect the buyer‚Äôs freedom of choice.

Think of it like ordering 10 items online and receiving something different. The law tells you exactly what you can do.

Meaning of Delivery of Wrong Quantity

Delivery of wrong quantity occurs when the seller delivers:

  • Less goods than agreed, or

  • More goods than agreed, or

  • Goods mixed with goods of a different description

Such delivery amounts to improper performance of the contract.

Rules Relating to Delivery of Wrong Quantity

1. Delivery of Less Quantity

If the seller delivers less quantity than agreed, the buyer has two clear options:

  1. Reject the goods completely, or

  2. Accept the goods delivered and pay for them at the contract price

üìå The buyer is not forced to accept less quantity.

Example

If 100 bags of cement are ordered but only 90 are delivered, the buyer may accept 90 and pay for them.

2. Delivery of Excess Quantity

If the seller delivers more quantity than agreed, the buyer has three options:

  1. Reject the excess goods, or

  2. Accept the agreed quantity and reject the rest, or

  3. Accept the whole delivery and pay for all goods at the contract rate

Example

Ordered 50 chairs but received 55:

  • Buyer may keep 50 only, or

  • Keep all 55 and pay for them

3. Delivery of Mixed Goods

If goods delivered are mixed with goods of a different description, the buyer may:

  1. Reject the whole delivery, or

  2. Accept the goods that match the contract and reject the rest

Example

Ordered pure basmati rice but received a mix of basmati and ordinary rice.

4. Buyer Is Not Bound to Accept Wrong Quantity

The buyer is not legally bound to accept goods delivered in wrong quantity.

The choice always lies with the buyer, not the seller.

5. Effect of Acceptance of Wrong Quantity

If the buyer chooses to accept:

  • Less goods, or

  • Excess goods, or

  • Part of mixed goods

Then the buyer must pay for the accepted goods at the contract price.

6. Slight Excess or Shortage (Trade Usage)

If the contract or trade practice allows slight variation in quantity, the buyer may not reject the goods.

Example

In bulk goods like grains or coal, small variations are sometimes accepted.

7. Intention of the Parties Matters

Courts also look at the intention of the buyer and seller:

  • If intention was strict quantity ‚Üí buyer may reject

  • If intention allowed flexibility ‚Üí rejection may not be allowed

A hotel orders 20 kg of sugar:

  • Seller delivers 22 kg

The hotel may:

  • Return extra 2 kg, or

  • Keep all 22 kg and pay for them

The decision is entirely the buyer‚Äôs.

Importance of These Rules

  • Protect buyers from forced acceptance

  • Encourage accurate delivery by sellers

  • Reduce disputes in business

  • Promote fairness in trade

Advantages of Buyer‚Äôs Protection

  • Buyer gets freedom of choice

  • Seller becomes more careful

  • Ensures balance in contracts

Final Takeaway (Conclusion)

The rules relating to delivery of wrong quantity ensure that no buyer is forced to accept something he did not order.
They give full control to the buyer to accept, reject, or partially accept goods depending on the situation.

In one strong line:

Wrong delivery never forces wrong acceptance.
